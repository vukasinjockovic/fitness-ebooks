# Key Quotes: The Muscle and Strength Pyramid: Nutrition

## Source
- **Book**: The Muscle and Strength Pyramid: Nutrition
- **Authors**: Eric Helms, Andrea Valdez, Andy Morgan
- **Edition**: Second Edition (2018)

---

## On The Big Picture

> "There's no such thing as a good or bad food, but there is such a thing as a good or bad diet."

> "I can't tell you how many articles I run into with titles like '10 Foods That Will Get You Ripped!' or '10 Bad Foods That Bodybuilders Should Always Avoid.' These articles jump right into food choices without any mention of what your calorie intake should be."

> "The problem is that we don't eat food in isolation. We have diets."

---

## On Prioritization

> "If you've already screwed up Level 1, you will inevitably have already screwed up your macronutrient distribution as well."

> "Too often, information in Level 4 or 5 is delivered without context in the fitness world and this creates a lot of confusion."

> "This expression of 'putting the cart before the horse' means that you are focusing on the details before you know the context of those details and the big picture."

---

## On Flexibility and Consistency

> "We want to be only as accurate as we need to be in order to be consistent enough that we can get to our goals."

> "Different goals are going to require different levels of accuracy, but they all require consistency."

> "In research, dietary restraint is highly associated with folks who can lose weight, but flexible dietary restraint is associated with those who lose weight, keep it off, and stay sane while doing so."

> "You can have a lot of theory and a lot of knowledge and still never get to where you want to go if you don't know how to apply those concepts consistently and how to make them a part of your lifestyle and behavior."

---

## On All-or-Nothing Thinking

> "A meal plan might direct you to have an apple at noon, and instead you have a banana. If you view a meal plan as an immutable plan that must be followed, you may feel like you screwed up, get down on yourself, and because you see the day as already lost, eat a whole large pizza."

> "The mediating factor between flexible and rigid dieters is whether or not you think of food in a black and white, all or nothing manner."

> "In a culture where 'if it fits your macros' or 'follow a meal plan and eat clean all the time' are often presented as the only two ways of doing things, it seems like you're stuck with two rigid options."

---

## On Tracking and Internal Cues

> "After you spend years 'going by the numbers' and modifying your body composition using the quantitative approaches outlined in this guide, it is actually very important to then learn how to once again listen to your body."

> "Regularly self-weighing and tracking calories and macros are associated with, and could potentially exacerbate, body image and eating disorders."

> "The more one relies on external cues to dictate energy intake and eating behavior, the more they must consequently ignore natural, internal cues of hunger and satiety, which can have lasting consequences."

---

## On Supplements

> "Supplements are the least important part of The Pyramid and are not necessary for success, barring that you do not have some type of nutritional deficiency or condition that requires them."

> "The vast majority of supplements on the market don't do anything except burn a hole in your wallet."

> "Even the most effective supplements that have been tried and tested time and time again for decades that can help you reach your goals, make only a very small impact. Likely, the impact they will make might not even be discernible to you."

> "1000s of supplements have come out over the years, and 99.9% of them have disappeared because they proved useless."

---

## On Rate of Weight Change

> "Someone who is eating a lower protein intake than I would recommend, but who is dieting and losing 0.5 to 1% of their body weight per week, is likely going to maintain more muscle mass than someone eating as much protein as I recommend while losing 1.5 to 2% of their body weight per week."

> "In order to truly see a change in fat mass of a pound in a single day would require a deficit or a surplus of ~3500 kcal."

> "The day to day up and down fluctuations in your body weight, which often meet or exceed a pound, are due to changes in water weight and food bulk in the gut."

---

## On Metabolic Adaptation

> "Metabolic adaptation (also known as adaptive thermogenesis) is the process by which your body reduces its energy expenditure over the course of a diet, to try to slow down your weight-loss. This is a survival mechanism and the normal response to dieting."

> "NEAT is one of the main ways we regulate energy expenditure... a lot of that is a function of just fidgeting, postural support and control, and subconscious movement. It's not something we can really modify."

> "Some people will sit at their desk all day and burn a whole lot more calories than I ever will, simply because I have a low NEAT."

---

## On Recovery Diet vs. Reverse Diet

> "What reverse dieting doesn't acknowledge, is that the fastest way to reverse these adaptations is to not only increase calories but also to gain weight."

> "In its more extreme iterations, the reverse diet may result in you spending many weeks in an energy deficit, even after your diet, before you are in a surplus."

> "Being shredded is not conducive to gaining muscle mass, it's only conducive to doing well on a bodybuilding stage. The only time you need to be shredded is when you're on stage."

> "Ironically, your desire to keep your newly attained leanness can become a barrier to you looking your best in future competitions."

---

## On Contest Prep

> "95–99% of the improvement in appearance seen at the end of a contest prep when a competitor is on stage depends on the years of training leading up to the show and the long diet immediately preceding it."

> "Given equal muscularity and symmetry, a competitor who peaks perfectly but is not appropriately lean, will be beaten by a shredded competitor who peaks poorly."

> "Competitive bodybuilding is an extreme sport, and thus, even when approached in a flexible manner, it takes a relatively extreme level of commitment to achieve success on stage."

---

## On Water/Sodium Manipulation

> "I have never personally seen a natural competitor improve their appearance with cutting water, cutting sodium and/or loading potassium. At best, I've known competitors who don't harm their physique by doing this."

> "By cutting sodium prior to getting on stage, you are giving yourself a double whammy of decreased blood pressure and not being able to get a pump."

---

## On Body Fat Testing

> "A body-fat percentage is largely useless information (yes, you read that correctly). If you care about how you look, or if you are a physique competitor, you aren't going for a number; you're going for a look."

> "When properly done, a DXA assessment can't typically get below a ± 2% error rate for repeated body fat percentage assessment within the same individual."

---

## On Diet Breaks

> "The concept of a diet break is essentially just getting your oil changed before your car breaks down. Meaning, instead of dieting until you lose your mind and binge-eat, you simply implement a planned break to avoid the potential negative side effects."

> "Psychologically, this puts you in control of the process versus you breaking down as a consequence of the diet."

---

## On Coaching and Precision

> "Don't make less than 5g macro changes, it gives clients the impression they should be that precise and that <5g changes matter, which encourages unnecessary neuroticism."

---

## On The Path Forward

> "An analogy I often use to describe the different nutritional needs of an off-season bodybuilder or powerlifter compared to a bodybuilder during contest prep or a powerlifter dieting to a lighter weight class, is that the former is walking a wide path while the latter is walking an ever-narrowing path slowly becoming a tightrope."

> "The goal of integrating the awareness of your hunger and satiety levels with your newfound nutritional knowledge and experience is to eventually develop an approach that requires minimal day to day effort."
