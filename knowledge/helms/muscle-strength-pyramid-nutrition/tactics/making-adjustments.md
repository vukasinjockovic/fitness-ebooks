# Making Adjustments and Measuring Progress

## Source
- **Book**: The Muscle and Strength Pyramid: Nutrition
- **Author**: Eric Helms, PhD
- **Context**: Post-setup chapter on diet management

## Overview

Setting up a nutrition plan is only the beginning. Knowing how to assess progress and make adjustments is equally important for achieving body composition goals.

## Assessment Methods

### 1. Scale Weight

**Appropriate For:**
- Weight-class athletes dieting to make weight
- Physique competitors (most prep stages)
- Non-competitors comfortable with scale

**Key Principles:**

**Weigh Consistently:**
1. Immediately upon waking
2. After using the toilet
3. On flat, hard surface (not carpet)
4. Nude
5. At least 3x per week (daily is better)

**Use Averages:**
> "The key to effectively using the scale is to weigh-in at least three times per week (and if only three times per week, once on a weekend day)."

**Calculate weekly averages to eliminate noise from:**
- Water fluctuations
- Food bulk in gut
- Sodium intake variations
- Menstrual cycle (women)

**Timeframe for Assessment:**
- Ignore Week 1 (water/glycogen shifts)
- Compare at least 2 weeks of averages
- Women: may need 3 weeks (menstrual cycle)
- Gaining: Monthly comparisons needed (slower changes)

### 2. Visual Assessment

**Appropriate For:**
- Physique competitors (especially late prep)
- Adjunct to other methods
- Assessing quality of weight loss

**Key Principles:**

**Take Photos/Videos:**
- Same location each time
- Same lighting (light facing you, camera between)
- Same time of day
- Multiple poses

> "Don't just look in the mirror and go off memory. Actually take a short video of you going through repeated poses, or take pictures."

**Limitations:**
- Not useful when body fat is high (changes less visible)
- Not useful during gaining (you may look "worse" while gaining muscle)
- Subjective; get second opinion

> "The higher in body fat you are, the less obvious visual changes will be. On the flip side, the leaner you are, the more obvious they will be."

### 3. Performance Assessment

**Appropriate For:**
- Everyone at various phases
- Indirect measure of muscle preservation/gain

**What to Track:**
- Strength on key lifts
- Volume capacity (work done per session)
- Relative strength (for weight-class athletes)

**Expected Patterns:**

| Phase | Strength Expectation |
|-------|---------------------|
| Early cut | May still gain |
| Mid cut | Maintain |
| Late cut (lean) | Minimize losses |
| Gaining | Progressive gains |

> "Many people say you can't gain strength while cutting, but that's false. In initial diet stages, especially if you aren't lean to start, you can definitely gain strength."

### 4. Body Circumference Measurements

**Appropriate For:**
- Fat loss (alternative to scale)
- Those with negative scale relationship
- Detecting recomp (simultaneous fat loss/muscle gain)

**Advantages:**
- Shows smaller changes more reliably
- Not affected by water fluctuations
- Can show progress when scale doesn't move

## Why Body Fat Testing is Problematic

### DXA Limitations

> "When properly done, a DXA assessment can't typically get below a ± 2% error rate for repeated body fat percentage assessment."

**With suboptimal conditions:**
- Error can reach ±10%
- Requires same time of day
- Same proximity to training
- Same hydration status
- Same dietary conditions
- Same menstrual phase (females)

### The Irony

Skinfold calipers, often considered "less accurate," are actually **more reliable** when:
- Used by trained anthropometrist
- Using sum of skinfolds (not converting to body fat %)
- Same person measures each time

> "If you simply use the sum of skinfolds alone, it is actually highly reliable when assessed by a trained anthropometrist."

### Body Fat % is Largely Useless

> "Yes, you read that correctly... If you care about how you look, or if you are a physique competitor, you aren't going for a number; you're going for a look."

## Making Adjustments

### Initial Check (Week 1)

**Did weight move in the right direction?**

- Cutting: Weight should decrease
- Gaining: Weight should increase

**If not:**
> "Recalculate your calories with an increased or decreased activity multiplier of 0.2."

Example: Used 1.5 multiplier, no loss → try 1.3

### Ongoing Adjustments (After Week 1)

**When to Adjust:**
- Rate falls outside target (0.5-1% loss/week or 0.5-1.5% gain/month)
- Consistently for 2+ weeks

**How Much to Adjust:**

| Calorie Intake | Adjustment Size |
|----------------|-----------------|
| Under 3000 | 100-150 kcal |
| Over 3000 | 150-200 kcal |

**Macro Adjustment Options:**

| Target Change | Option 1 | Option 2 | Option 3 |
|--------------|----------|----------|----------|
| ~100 kcal | 25g carbs | 10g fat | 15g carbs, 5g fat |
| ~150 kcal | 40g carbs | 15g fat | 25g carbs, 5g fat |
| ~200 kcal | 50g carbs | 20g fat | 25g carbs, 10g fat |

**Rules:**
- Round to nearest 5g
- Don't go below minimums (0.25g/lb fat, 0.5g/lb carbs)
- Can reduce either or both carbs/fat

> "Don't make less than 5g macro changes, it gives clients the impression they should be that precise."

### Cardio as Adjustment

**Alternative to food reduction:**
- Add 40 min LISS session, OR
- Add 20 min HIIT session (max 1-2x/week)

**Limit:**
> "You don't want your total time doing cardio to exceed half your time spent resistance training."

### Common Cause of Stalls

> "Miscounting calories is incredibly common, and often the cause of stalls."

Before adjusting plan, verify tracking accuracy.

## Phase-Specific Assessment

### Early Prep (Physique)

**Priority:** Rate of weight loss
**Secondary:** Strength, visual
**Expected:** Progress in all three

### Mid Prep

**Priority:** Weight loss closer to 0.5%/week
**Secondary:** Maintain strength/volume
**Visual:** Becoming more important

### Late Prep

**Priority:** Visual changes
**Secondary:** Mitigate performance loss
**Scale:** Secondary to appearance

### Gaining Phase

**Visual:** Not useful (may look "worse")
**Performance:** Primary indicator
**Scale:** Monthly trends

...      | ...    |           |

> "In the above table, the only relevant information are the averages in the left column."
