# Carb Loading and Peak Week

## Source
- **Book**: The Muscle and Strength Pyramid: Nutrition
- **Author**: Eric Helms, PhD
- **Context**: Peaking for Competition chapter

## Overview

Carbohydrate loading for physique competition is the strategic manipulation of carb intake to maximize muscle fullness while minimizing subcutaneous water retention. This is distinct from endurance athlete carb loading.

## Fundamental Principle

> "95–99% of the improvement in appearance seen at the end of a contest prep when a competitor is on stage depends on the years of training leading up to the show and the long diet immediately preceding it."

Peak week makes only a 1-5% difference. Get lean first.

## The Physiology

### Glycogen Storage

- Carbohydrate stored primarily in skeletal muscle
- More glycogen = fuller, larger-appearing muscles
- Glycogen also holds water (beneficial intramuscularly)

### The "Spilling Over" Problem

> "Spilling over is the loss of definition when one eats too many carbs in an acute window. The muscles appear big and full, but the hard-earned separation and detail is blurred."

Too many carbs → excess water → blurred definition

### Why Bodybuilder Carb Loading Differs

**Endurance Athletes:**
- Habitually high-carb diet
- Load at 12g/kg (5-6g/lb)
- Don't care about appearance

**Physique Athletes:**
- Chronically low-carb during prep
- Storage capacity reduced over time
- Must avoid spillover
- Often 1-3g/kg during prep

> "In a human being, the glycogen 'gas tank' storage capacity will effectively decrease in size as enzymes downregulate over the course of prep."

## Eating Up Into Your Show

### The Ideal Approach

Be stage-ready 3-4 weeks out, then:
- Increase calories ~10%/week
- Reduce cardio each week
- Arrive at peak week fuller and healthier

### Why This Works

- More predictable carb response
- Less depleted = more storage capacity
- Physiologically healthier
- Psychologically easier
- Makes peak week simpler

### Example Protocol

**4 weeks out (achieved condition):**
- Low days: P: 150g, C: 100g, F: 40g
- Refeed days: P: 150g, C: 150g, F: 40g
- Cardio: 4x45min LISS/week

**Each week, increase:**
- Carbs: +10g
- Fat: +5g
- Cardio: -1 session

**1 week out (entering peak week):**
- Low days: P: 150g, C: 130g, F: 55g
- Refeed days: P: 150g, C: 180g, F: 55g
- Cardio: 1x45min LISS/week

## Loading Strategies

### Back Loading

**Definition:** Single load in the 1-2 days before competition

**When to Use:**

1. **Need more diet time:**
> "A front load means five or more days out of a deficit, while a back load only takes one or two days."

2. **After eating up into show:**
> "The best-case scenario is getting ready early, eating up into your show, and running a back load."

**Implementation:**
- Track response to refeeds during prep
- Note which day you look best (day of, +1 day, +2 days)
- Time your competition refeed accordingly

### Front Loading

**Definition:** Two peaks - earlier in week, taper down, then load again before show

**Rationale:**
> "The first peak 'stretches' your tank (upregulating enzymes), while the second allows you to get fuller than you could have previously."

**When to Use:**

1. **Ready on time but not early:**
- Need the double peak for fullness
- More depleted than ideal

2. **Want correction opportunity:**
> "In a front load, you have multiple days to modulate the load if you are fuller or flatter than expected."

**Advantage:**
- Can adjust mid-week based on appearance
- More forgiving of errors

### Comparison

| Aspect | Back Load | Front Load |
|--------|-----------|------------|
| Complexity | Simple | More complex |
| Days out of deficit | 1-2 | 5+ |
| Correction opportunity | Minimal | Multiple days |
| Best for | Eating-up scenario | Ready on-time scenario |
| Risk | May look flat | Spillover if miscalculated |

## Water and Sodium: What NOT to Do

### Traditional (Problematic) Approach

- Cut water intake
- Cut sodium
- Load potassium

### Why It Doesn't Work for Natural Athletes

**Dehydration Reduces Muscle Water:**
> "When you are dehydrated, muscle water decreases as well, which can make you look smaller and flat."

**Sodium Affects Blood Pressure:**
> "By cutting sodium prior to getting on stage, you are giving yourself a double whammy of decreased blood pressure and not being able to get a pump."

**Carb Absorption Requires Sodium:**
> "Glucose transport from the small intestine requires sodium. So you've also hampered the effectiveness of your carb load."

### The Triple Whammy

Cut water + cut sodium =
1. Decreased total body water
2. Can't get a pump
3. Impaired carb absorption

### Eric's Recommendation

> "If anything, it might make sense to load sodium immediately prior to getting on stage to keep vascular pressure high and aid your pump up."

**Don't:**
- Cut water
- Cut sodium
- Load potassium

### Why These Practices Persist

> "Back in the day, bodybuilders didn't have access to less androgenic drugs, drugs to control estrogen, and diuretics."

Originated with enhanced athletes dealing with drug-induced water retention. Not applicable to natural competitors.

## Tracking Your Response

### During Prep (6-8 weeks out)

**Take photos and notes:**
- Day of refeed
- 1 day after
- 2 days after

**Find your pattern:**
- Some look best day-of
- Some need 1-2 days to "harden up"
- Some need back-to-back refeeds

### Be Objective

> "Don't just look in the mirror and get a feeling, look at the pictures (or better yet video) and ideally have someone else give you their opinion too."

### Common Mistake

> "I've seen competitors convinced they look best two days after their refeed when they look super flat and stringy to me because they were hyper-focused on an ab vein that only they could see."

Get outside perspective.

## Training Considerations

### Week of Show

**Goals:**
- Maintain stimulus for glycogen storage
- Avoid excessive fatigue/soreness
- Don't damage muscle

**Recommendations:**
- Still train hard
- Avoid novel exercises
- May reduce leg work (competition posing)
- Taper volume, not intensity

## Summary: Best Practice

### Scenario A: Got Ready Early (Ideal)

1. Eat up 3-4 weeks out
2. Arrive at peak week fuller
3. Run simple back load
4. Don't manipulate water/sodium

### Scenario B: Ready On Time

1. Run front load for double peak
2. Track appearance mid-week
3. Adjust if needed
4. Don't manipulate water/sodium

### Scenario C: Not Quite Ready (Least Ideal)

1. Run back load to save diet time
2. Accept may look a bit flat
3. Consider if show timing is wrong
4. Don't manipulate water/sodium
