# Supplement Evaluation

## Source
- **Book**: The Muscle and Strength Pyramid: Nutrition
- **Author**: Eric Helms, PhD
- **Context**: Level 5 of the Nutrition Pyramid - Supplementation

## Overview

Supplements are the least important level of the Nutrition Pyramid. This framework provides tools for evaluating supplements based on quality, validity, and effectiveness.

## Core Philosophy

> "Supplements are the least important part of The Pyramid and are not necessary for success, barring that you do not have some type of nutritional deficiency or condition that requires them."

> "The vast majority of supplements on the market don't do anything except burn a hole in your wallet."

## The Three Evaluation Criteria

### 1. Quality

**Question:** Does the product actually contain what the label says?

**Why It Matters:**
> "A surprising number of products simply don't contain the quality or the amount of the active ingredients that their labels claim and some actually have ingredients in them that should not be there at all or that are either unsafe, illegal, banned, or all of the above."

**How to Ensure Quality:**

**Third-Party Testing:**
- Look for lab analysis reports
- Stamps like "Certified Drug Free," "Certified for Sport," "Informed Choice"
- Organizations: USP, NSF International

**Licensed Ingredients:**
- CreaPure (creatine monohydrate)
- CarnoSyn (beta-alanine)
- These indicate verified quality from reputable suppliers

**Avoid Proprietary Blends:**
> "A proprietary blend is where a company trademarks a combination of substances that they mix together and then gives that mixture a name of their choosing. They are required to list the ingredients, but not the dosages."

**Problem:** Often used to hide underdosing of active ingredients

### 2. Validity

**Question:** Does this supplement have any real effect?

**Resources for Verification:**
- **Examine.com** - Best comprehensive supplement database
- **JISSN** (Journal of International Society of Sports Nutrition) - Open-access research

**Human Effect Matrix (Examine.com):**
- Shows effect direction (increase/decrease)
- Magnitude of effect
- Consistency across studies
- Quality of evidence

### 3. Effectiveness

**Question:** Is the effect relevant to YOUR goals and significant enough to matter?

**Considerations:**
- Does it affect muscle gain, fat loss, or performance?
- Is the effect size meaningful in practice?
- Is it cost-effective?

> "Some supplements actually have an effect, but it's just so insignificant that it's not worthwhile to purchase. Other supplements have an effect, but the effects themselves are irrelevant to the goals of building muscle or strength."

## The A/B/C Tier System

### A-Tier: Strong Evidence, Broadly Relevant

**Multivitamins**
- One-a-day basic formula (not mega-dosed)
- Nutritional "safety net" especially when cutting
- Get blood work to check for deficiencies first

**Fish Oil (EPA/DHA)**
- 1-2g combined EPA/DHA daily
- Health benefits: depression, cardiac, blood pressure, waist circumference
- May need 3-6g fish oil to get 1-2g EPA/DHA
- Alternative: Algae oil for vegans

**Vitamin D3**
- Especially if limited sun exposure
- Common deficiency in indoor athletes
- Correct insufficiency for immune function

**Creatine Monohydrate**
- 3-5g daily
- Most researched supplement
- Improves strength, power, muscle gain
- No loading needed; consistent daily intake works

**Caffeine**
- 3-6mg/kg body weight
- Improves performance, focus
- Tolerance develops; cycle if desired

### B-Tier: Situational or Mixed Evidence

**Beta-Alanine**
- Helps with muscular endurance
- The "tingles" are harmless
- Most beneficial for higher rep work

**Citrulline Malate**
- May improve blood flow and endurance
- Added in Second Edition

**Protein Powder**
- NOT magical; just convenient protein
- Not superior to food protein
- Useful if struggling to hit protein targets

### C-Tier: Not Worth Your Time

> "If a supplement you are considering is not mentioned, consider it to belong in this category."

**Examples of popular but ineffective supplements:**
- Most testosterone boosters
- Most fat burners
- Most "muscle builders" beyond basics

**D-Aspartic Acid Case Study:**
> "A study came out in 2009 that found it increased testosterone production in men to a rather impressive degree. Immediately, supplement companies jumped on the bandwagon... then to their surprise... a study came out in 2013 that found D-Aspartic acid not only failed to increase testosterone levels but most importantly it had no effect on body composition or strength. A 2015 study came out that actually found D-Aspartic acid decreased testosterone levels!"

## The Protein Powder Scam

### Protein Spiking

**The Problem:**
- Labs test nitrogen content, not amino acid profile
- Companies add cheap amino acids (glycine, taurine)
- Or non-protein nitrogen sources (creatine, beta-alanine)
- Product appears to have more protein than it does

### Red Flags

1. **Price too good to be true** - Whey is a commodity; significant discounts indicate quality issues
2. **Proprietary blends** - Hiding individual amino amounts
3. **Low leucine content** - Should be ~11% of protein (2.7g per 25g protein)

### Safe Choice

70:30 pea:rice protein blend mimics whey amino profile for vegans.

## Be a Late Adopter

### Why Wait on New Supplements

> "The smart thing to do would be to wait until multiple groups of researchers have confirmed the findings of the first group to ensure that the findings are legit."

**Reasons:**
1. Single studies can be false positives
2. Researcher bias exists
3. Industry funding may suppress negative findings
4. Small sample sizes unreliable

> "1000s of supplements have come out over the years, and 99.9% of them have disappeared because they proved useless."

### Publication Bias

> "When supplement companies fund research, they sometimes include in the contract a clause that states they have the right to decide whether or not the data is published."

Positive studies get published; null findings get buried.

## Practical Framework

### Before Buying Any Supplement

1. **Is it necessary?** Can you get this from food?
2. **Is it quality-verified?** Third-party testing?
3. **Is there evidence?** Check Examine.com
4. **Is it relevant?** Does it actually help YOUR goals?
5. **Is it cost-effective?** Worth the money?
6. **Is it established?** Multiple confirming studies?

### Minimum Effective Stack

For most lifters:
1. Creatine (3-5g/day)
2. Vitamin D (if deficient/low sun)
3. Fish oil (1-2g EPA/DHA)
4. Caffeine (optional, pre-workout)

Everything else is truly optional.
