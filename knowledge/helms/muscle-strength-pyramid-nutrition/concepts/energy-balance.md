# Energy Balance

## Source
- **Book**: The Muscle and Strength Pyramid: Nutrition
- **Author**: Eric Helms, PhD
- **Context**: Level 1 of the Nutrition Pyramid - The Foundation

## Overview

Energy balance is the relationship between calories consumed and calories expended. It is the single most important factor in determining body weight changes and forms the foundation of the Nutrition Pyramid.

## Core Principle

> "If you've already screwed up Level 1, you will inevitably have already screwed up your macronutrient distribution as well."

No amount of optimization at higher levels can compensate for incorrect energy balance.

## Finding Maintenance Calories

### Method 1: Tracking (Preferred)

**Process:**
1. Gather tools: digital body weight scale, digital food scale, tracking app
2. Track food intake for 2 weeks (as accurately as possible)
3. Weigh yourself every morning (same conditions)
4. Calculate average daily intake
5. Calculate weekly weight averages (Week 1 vs Week 2)
6. Use the 3500 calorie rule to estimate surplus/deficit

**Example Calculation:**
- Average intake: 2100 calories
- Week 1 average weight: 142.1 lbs
- Week 2 average weight: 142.3 lbs
- Weight gained: 0.2 lbs
- Surplus: 0.2 × 3500 = 700 calories/week = 100 cal/day
- Therefore: Maintenance ≈ 2000 calories (2100 - 100)

**When NOT to use this method:**
- Just started training (muscle gain confounds results)
- Coming back from training layoff
- Weight highly unstable for other reasons

### Method 2: Calculation (Estimate)

**Formula:**
```
Maintenance = (Body weight in lbs × 10) × Activity Multiplier
OR
Maintenance = (Body weight in kg × 22) × Activity Multiplier
```

**Activity Multipliers:**
| Activity Level | Multiplier |
|---------------|------------|
| Sedentary | 1.2-1.3 |
| Lightly Active | 1.4-1.5 |
| Moderately Active | 1.6-1.7 |
| Very Active | 1.8-1.9 |
| Extremely Active | 2.0+ |

**Note on NEAT:**
> "Some people will sit at their desk all day and burn a whole lot more calories than I ever will, simply because I have a low NEAT."

NEAT (Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis) varies greatly between individuals and is largely subconscious (fidgeting, posture, etc.).

## Rates of Weight Change

### Fat Loss Recommendations

**Target Rate:** 0.5-1% of body weight per week

**Why This Range:**
- Preserves lean body mass
- Maintains training performance
- Sustainable adherence
- Minimizes metabolic adaptation

**Too Fast (>1%/week):**
- Greater lean mass loss
- Performance degradation
- Higher hunger/fatigue
- Worse adherence
- More severe metabolic adaptation

> "Someone who is eating a lower protein intake than I would recommend, but who is dieting and losing 0.5 to 1% of their body weight per week, is likely going to maintain more muscle mass than someone eating as much protein as I recommend while losing 1.5 to 2% of their body weight per week."

### Weight Gain Recommendations

**Target Rate:** 0.5-1.5% of body weight per month

**Why Slower for Gaining:**
- Muscle gain is slow (natural limit ~0.5 lb/week for beginners)
- Excess surplus → excess fat gain
- No benefit to gaining faster than you can build muscle

**Who Can Gain Faster:**
- Complete beginners (high adaptation potential)
- Returning from layoff ("muscle memory")
- Those starting underweight

## Should I Cut or Gain?

### Factors to Consider

**Cut First If:**
- Higher body fat (males >15%, females >25%)
- Better insulin sensitivity when leaner
- Longer productive gaining phase ahead
- Psychological preference for leanness

**Gain First If:**
- Already lean (males <12%, females <20%)
- Underdeveloped muscle base
- Performance priorities
- Psychological need for progress

### The Newbie Exception

> "Someone who is a rank beginner (or returning to training after a period of detraining) will make progress in both areas at the same time due to having a low threshold for adaptation."

Beginners can often:
- Gain 5-10% body weight without increasing body fat %
- Lose fat while gaining muscle simultaneously
- Recomp effectively before needing dedicated phases

## Energy Availability

### Definition

Energy availability = Energy intake - Exercise energy expenditure

### Signs of Low Energy Availability

**In Women:**
- Loss of menstrual cycle (amenorrhea)
- "Female Athlete Triad"
- Bone density concerns

**In Men:**
- Testosterone decline
- Reduced libido
- Mood disturbance

**General Signs:**
- Sleep disruption
- Elevated stress hormones
- Elevated hunger hormones
- Reduced metabolic rate
- Fatigue
- Preoccupation with food

> "To push oneself to this point, below a body-fat range that your body 'wants' to have from a biological perspective and past what would be considered physically healthy, requires a long and intensive diet."

## Practical Differences: Cutting vs Gaining

### Cutting

- Tighter margins for error
- More precision needed
- Performance may decline
- Hunger signals unreliable (too high)
- Need to ignore internal cues

### Gaining

- More forgiveness in tracking
- Less precision required
- Performance should improve
- Satiety signals may be high
- Can rely more on internal cues

## The 3500 Calorie Rule

**Principle:** 1 lb of adipose tissue ≈ 3500 calories

**Limitations:**
- Not all weight change is fat
- Energy expenditure adapts over time
- Best for short-term estimations

**Best Use:**
- Initial 2-week maintenance assessment
- Short-term surplus/deficit calculations
- Ballpark estimates

> "The '3500 calorie rule' isn't perfect since not all weight gained or lost is fat and energy expenditure changes over time, but this value is still a good way to roughly estimate maintenance calories."
