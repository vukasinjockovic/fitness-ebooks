# Fitness-Fatigue Model

**Type:** framework
**Status:** solid
**Last Updated:** 2026-02-03
**Aliases:** Two-Factor Model, Fitness-Fatigue Paradigm, Banister Model

## Summary

The Fitness-Fatigue Model explains why more training isn't always better. Both fitness and fatigue are generated from training; fatigue masks fitness and therefore affects performance. Performance equals fitness minus fatigue. This model underpins all periodization strategies and explains the need for deloads, tapers, and recovery periods.

## Core Components

### Fitness
- Physical capability achieved as a result of training
- Increases gradually over time with effective training
- Relatively slow to build, slow to decay

### Fatigue
- Generated in proportion to workload and intensity
- Affected by fatigue resistance (work capacity)
- Can be hindered by external factors: sleep, nutrition, life stress
- Faster to build, faster to decay than fitness

### Performance
- **Performance = Fitness - Fatigue**
- When fresh: performance approaches fitness level
- When fatigued: performance is suppressed below fitness level

## The U-Shaped Volume-Progress Relationship

```
Progress
   ^
   |     *
   |   *   *
   |  *     *
   | *       *
   |*         *
   +-----------> Volume
     Optimal zone
```

- Initial sets give "more bang for your buck"
- Returns diminish as volume increases
- Past a point, more volume = less progress
- Eventually, regression can occur (overtraining)

## Training States

| State | Description | Recovery Time |
|-------|-------------|---------------|
| Normal adaptation | Fatigue managed within range | Ongoing |
| Functional overreaching | Planned overload, recoverable | Days to 1-2 weeks |
| Non-functional overreaching | Unplanned, returns to baseline only | 1-2 weeks |
| Overtraining | Chronic maladaptation | Weeks to months |

## Practical Implications

### Why Deloads Work
- Fatigue dissipates faster than fitness decays
- Reducing volume lets fatigue drop while maintaining fitness
- Performance rebounds, often to new heights

### Why More Isn't Always Better
Studies cited by Helms:
- Gonzalez-Badillo (2005): Moderate volume group progressed most
- Heaselgrave (2018): 18 sets/week beat both 9 and 27 sets for biceps
- Very high volume groups showed no progress or regression

### External Stress Matters
> "People who experience more negative life occurrences in the course of a training program don't adapt to it as well."
> — Citing Bartholomew et al., 2008
