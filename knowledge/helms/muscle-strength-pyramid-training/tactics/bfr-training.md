# Blood Flow Restriction (BFR) Training

**Type:** tactic
**Status:** solid
**Last Updated:** 2026-02-03
**Aliases:** Occlusion Training, KAATSU, BFR

## Summary

Blood Flow Restriction training uses a cuff, wrap, or band at the proximal limb (upper thigh or upper arm) to restrict venous blood flow while maintaining arterial flow during exercise. This allows very light loads (20-30% 1RM) to produce comparable hypertrophy to heavier loads, making it valuable for training around joint pain or injury without losing muscle stimulus.

## Mechanism

**How it works:**
- Wrap restricts venous return (blood leaving muscle)
- Arterial flow (blood entering muscle) maintained
- Metabolites accumulate in working muscle
- Earlier recruitment and fatigue of muscle fibers
- Hypertrophy stimulus achieved with much lighter loads

**What it produces:**
- Similar hypertrophy to heavier loads (meta-analysis confirmed)
- Does NOT produce strength gains equivalent to heavy training
- Useful for maintaining/building muscle when heavy loading not possible

## When to Use BFR

### Primary Use Case: Joint Issues
> "This can be very useful when experiencing joint pain in the knees or elbows as you can maintain a muscular stimulus, with a load much lower than normal which may facilitate joint recovery."
> — Helms, Training Pyramid, Level 3

**Scenario:** Only "aches and pains" question positive in recovery assessment
- Normal volume and RPE
- High-rep week (12-20) if sufficient
- If still painful: BFR on single-joint movements

### Other Applications
- Injury rehabilitation
- Travel/minimal equipment
- Volume accumulation without joint stress
- Elderly populations
- Post-surgery recovery

## How to Implement BFR

### Equipment
- Blood pressure cuff (gold standard)
- Knee wraps (practical/accessible)
- Elastic bandages
- Specialized BFR straps

### Placement
- **Lower body:** Upper thigh (near hip crease)
- **Upper body:** Upper arm (near armpit)

### Tightness
**Target:** 7/10 perceived tightness

**Signs it's correct:**
- Muscle "pumps" quickly
- Burns more than usual
- No tingling in limbs
- No color change (purple = too tight)

> "There should be no tingling in your limb and your limb shouldn't change color (shouldn't turn purplish), and if either happens it's too tight."
> — Helms, Training Pyramid, Level 3

### Load and Reps
- **Load:** 20-30% of 1RM
- **Reps:** To failure (necessary for stimulus)
- **Sets:** Normally programmed number

### Best Exercises for BFR
Single-joint movements:
- Leg extensions
- Leg curls
- Bicep curls
- Tricep extensions
- Calf raises
- Lateral raises

Less ideal (but possible):
- Leg press
- Machine rows
- Machine presses

## Research Support

> "Training loads as low as 20–30% of 1RM to be just as effective for hypertrophy (but not strength) as heavier loads (but not more effective)."
> — Helms, Training Pyramid, Level 3

Meta-analysis (Lixandrão et al., 2018):
- Compared BFR low-load vs high-load training
- Similar muscle hypertrophy outcomes
- High-load superior for strength

## Safety Considerations

### DO:
- Start with light pressure and progress
- Keep sessions relatively short
- Release between sets if needed
- Use appropriate wrap width (wider = less pressure needed)

### DON'T:
- Wrap so tight you get numbness/tingling
- Use on people with cardiovascular issues
- Use if limb changes color
- Keep wrapped for extended periods

### Contraindications
- Deep vein thrombosis history
- Peripheral vascular disease
- Pregnancy
- Uncontrolled hypertension
- Recent surgery (without medical clearance)

## Practical Example

**Scenario:** Knee pain preventing normal leg training

**Normal prescription:** 3×10 leg extensions at 100 lbs

**BFR modification:**
- Wrap upper thighs at 7/10 tightness
- 3×failure with 25-30 lbs (20-30% 1RM)
- Maintain hypertrophy stimulus
- Allow knee to recover from heavy loading
