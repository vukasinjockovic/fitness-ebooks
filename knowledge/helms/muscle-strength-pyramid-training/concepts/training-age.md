# Training Age

**Type:** concept
**Status:** solid
**Last Updated:** 2026-02-03
**Aliases:** Lifting Age, Experience Level, Training Experience

## Summary

Training age categorizes lifters not by years in the gym or arbitrary strength standards, but by how quickly they can progress relative to their genetic potential. A lifter with 10 years of suboptimal training may still be functionally intermediate, while a genetically gifted lifter might reach "advanced" adaptation rates in 2 years. Training age determines appropriate programming complexity.

## Definition Criteria

> "It is most useful to categorize ourselves based on the length of time it takes to improve (strength), rather than an arbitrary strength standard or the length of time we have been lifting."
> — Helms, Training Pyramid, Level 3

**Not defined by:**
- Years lifting
- Absolute strength numbers
- Physique development
- Competition level

**Defined by:**
- Rate of possible progress
- Distance from genetic ceiling
- Response to training stimuli

## The Three Categories

### Novice
**Rate of Progress:** Session to session

**Characteristics:**
- Far from genetic limit
- Simple programming works
- Rapid strength gains
- Technique improving significantly
- Any reasonable program produces results

**Programming Implications:**
- Linear progression (add weight each session)
- Focus on learning compound movements
- Deload = 10% load reduction when stalled
- 2-3x/week full body or simple split

### Intermediate
**Rate of Progress:** Week to week

**Characteristics:**
- Moderate distance from genetic limit
- More systematic approach needed
- Progress still relatively consistent
- Technique mostly established

**Programming Implications:**
- Weekly wave loading or linear within mesocycles
- Periodization becomes important
- Planned deloads (half volume, similar intensity)
- Higher frequency/volume than novice

### Advanced
**Rate of Progress:** Month to month (or slower)

**Characteristics:**
- 80-90% of genetic potential reached
- Slow, hard-fought gains
- Progress not apparent week-to-week
- May only add 15-30 lbs to total per year

**Programming Implications:**
- Block periodization essential
- 6-12 week mesocycles between tests
- Systematic approach to plateau resolution
- High training age ≠ high performance level

## Important Distinction: Performance vs Training Age

> "I have trained lifters who developed natural, pro-caliber physiques in their first year of lifting, or who attained an elite total one weight class higher than the one they compete in after one year of training for powerlifting. Yes, in terms of performance these athletes are elite, but in terms of their personal genetic potential, they were still intermediates."
> — Helms, Training Pyramid, Level 3

**Key insight:** Someone can be world-class but still intermediate in training age if they haven't approached their genetic ceiling.

Conversely:
> "Some people just don't have the natural genetic talent to perform at an elite level compared to others in the field but are still advanced in terms of how close they are to their own potential."
> — Helms, Training Pyramid, Level 3

## Helms' Personal Example

After 14 years of training:
- ~1400 lb total at 198-205 lb bodyweight
- ~400 Wilks score (decent but not elite)
- Would place last at IPF worlds
- Still making slow progress (5 kg total increase over 3 years)
- Trains as "advanced" despite not being "elite"

## Progression Timeline (General)

| Stage | Duration | Total Progress |
|-------|----------|----------------|
| Novice | 0-12 months | Rapid gains |
| Intermediate | 1-3+ years | Steady gains |
| Advanced | 3+ years | Slow gains |

**Elite progression examples:**
- Natural pro bodybuilders: ~1 lb lean mass per competitive year
- Elite powerlifters: 15-30 lbs added to total per season
- Olympic lifters: 17-25 lbs to total per 4-year cycle

## Key Quotes

> "Nobody can get bigger and stronger forever and everyone has a genetic limit. In general, the closer we are to our limit; the slower progress will be."
> — Helms, Training Pyramid, Level 3

> "The further away someone is from their genetic limit; the more easily they will be able to gain strength."
> — Helms, Training Pyramid, Level 3
