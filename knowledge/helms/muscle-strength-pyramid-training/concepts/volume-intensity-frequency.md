# Volume, Intensity, Frequency (VIF)

**Type:** concept
**Status:** solid
**Last Updated:** 2026-02-03
**Aliases:** VIF, Training Variables Triad, Big Three Variables

## Summary

Volume, intensity, and frequency are the three interdependent variables that form the actual foundation of training programs. They cannot be considered in isolation—changing one affects the optimal values of the others. Helms places all three in the same pyramid level (Level 2) because they are "interrelated and inseparable."

## Definitions

### Volume
Total amount of work performed, best counted as **sets per muscle group/movement per week**.

Why sets (not volume load):
- Volume load (sets × reps × load) inflates with high reps
- 3×25×100 vs 3×10×140 = 7500 vs 4200 volume load, but similar stimulus
- Sets in appropriate rep/intensity ranges = best proxy for stimulus

**Recommendation**: 10-20 sets per muscle group/movement per week

### Intensity
Two types:
1. **Intensity of Load**: How heavy (% 1RM, RM)
2. **Intensity of Effort**: How hard (RPE, proximity to failure)

Measurement methods:
- Percentage of 1RM (limited by individual variation)
- Rep Max (RM) - more practical
- RPE based on Repetitions in Reserve (RIR) - most flexible

**Recommendations**:
- Strength: 2/3-3/4 volume in 1-6 rep range, rest in 6-15, at RPE 5-10
- Hypertrophy: 2/3-3/4 volume in 6-12 rep range, rest in 1-6 and 12-20, at RPE 5-10

### Frequency
How training stress is organized across the week.

Higher frequency benefits:
- Better skill acquisition (practice spread out)
- Better recovery (less volume per session)
- Meta-analyses show 2x/week per muscle superior to 1x/week

**Recommendation**: 2+ times per muscle group/movement per week

## The Interdependence

> "If you train at a very high intensity of effort, near to failure, it takes longer for muscle damage to subside... potentially impacting the frequency you can train with."

> "Lifting heavy makes each individual repetition more stressful, requires longer rest periods, and can make a session take longer, limiting the volume you can (and probably should) perform."

> "Doing 16 sets of the same movement in a single day would require you to either decrease volume or intensity... compared to doing 8 sets on two different days."

## Key Research Findings

### Volume
- Meta-analyses: 10+ sets optimal for hypertrophy, 5-12+ for strength
- Gonzalez-Badillo (2005): Moderate volume > high or low volume for strength
- Heaselgrave (2018): 18 sets/week > 9 or 27 sets for biceps hypertrophy

### Intensity
- Schoenfeld (2014): 3RM and 10RM produced equal hypertrophy
- But 3RM = more joint pain, more dropouts, longer sessions
- Lasevicius (2018): 20% 1RM too light; 40-80% 1RM all effective

### Frequency
- Schoenfeld (2015): Full body 3x/week > split routine for hypertrophy
- Raastad (2012): Norwegian powerlifters: 6x/week > 3x/week (same volume)
- Multiple meta-analyses: 2x/week per muscle > 1x/week

## Summary Recommendations

| Variable | Recommendation | Notes |
|----------|---------------|-------|
| Volume | 10-20 sets/muscle/week | Consider overlap, heavy warm-ups |
| Intensity (Strength) | 2/3-3/4 in 1-6 rep range | Rest in 6-15 rep range |
| Intensity (Hypertrophy) | 2/3-3/4 in 6-12 rep range | Some 1-6 and 12-20 |
| Effort | RPE 5-10 | Varies by phase |
| Frequency | 2+x/week per muscle | Balance with volume per session |
