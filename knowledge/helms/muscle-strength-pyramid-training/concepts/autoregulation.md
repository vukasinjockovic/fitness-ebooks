# Autoregulation

**Type:** concept
**Status:** solid
**Last Updated:** 2026-02-03
**Aliases:** Auto-regulation, Flexible Periodization, Reactive Training

## Summary

Autoregulation is structured flexibility in training that adjusts variables based on daily readiness rather than rigid pre-planned values. Research shows flexible periodization produces better outcomes than fixed plans. Autoregulation can apply to load (via RPE), exercise selection, deload timing, and even whether to train at all.

## Research Foundation

**Flexible vs Fixed Periodization (McNamara & Stearne, 2010):**
- Two groups, same total volume over 12 weeks
- Group 1: Three session options (easy/moderate/hard), chose daily based on energy
- Group 2: Pre-planned order regardless of feeling
- **Result:** Flexible group made greater strength gains

**Follow-up Study (Colquhoun et al., 2017):**
- Trained lifters chose weekly order of strength/hypertrophy/power based on readiness
- **Result:** Better adherence than fixed order group

## Key Insight

> "We know stress can affect progress in the gym. Secondly, we know that having a flexible approach to periodization can be superior to having a rigid one (not unlike dieting)."
> — Helms, Training Pyramid, Level 1

## Types of Autoregulation

### 1. Autoregulating Load
**Method:** Use RPE instead of fixed percentages

Example:
- Prescribed: 3×5 @ 8 RPE
- Day 1 (fresh): Use 315 lbs
- Day 2 (tired): Use 295 lbs
- Both sessions achieve intended stimulus

### 2. Autoregulating Session Order
**Method:** Do hardest planned workout on best-feeling days

Example:
- Planned: Mon (hard), Wed (easy), Fri (moderate)
- Monday feels terrible → Do Wednesday's easy session
- Wednesday feels great → Do Monday's hard session
- Same work done, better quality

### 3. Autoregulating Days Off
**Method:** Take unplanned rest when indicators suggest

Post-block assessment checklist:
- Dreading the gym?
- Sleep worse than normal?
- Loads/reps decreasing?
- Stress worse than normal?
- Aches and pains worse than normal?

**Yes to 0-1:** Start next mesocycle
**Yes to 2+:** Take deload week

### 4. Autoregulating Exercise Selection
**Method:** Substitute exercises based on joint health, equipment, feel

Example:
- Knee bothering you → Swap squats for leg press
- Shoulder impingement → Swap barbell bench for DB or machine
- Gym crowded → Alternative exercise that hits same pattern

### 5. Autoregulating Deloads
**Method:** Reactive deloading based on recovery markers vs fixed schedule

> "My preferred method of implementing deloads (in non-dieting conditions) is to use a checklist after each mesocycle progression to assess recovery."
> — Helms, Training Pyramid, Level 3

**Rule of thumb:** Even with reactive deloading, take one after 3 consecutive mesocycles regardless.

## Practical Guidelines

### When to Override Autoregulation

Sometimes push through:
- Important training block
- Competition prep timeline
- First exposure to new volume (expect discomfort)

### When Autoregulation is Critical

Always autoregulate when:
- High life stress period
- Sleep significantly disrupted
- Illness or injury present
- Multiple recovery markers negative

## Integration with Periodization

Autoregulation works **within** a periodized structure:
- Block periodization sets the phase (volume vs intensity)
- DUP sets the within-week variation
- Autoregulation adjusts day-to-day execution

Example: In a volume block, you might autoregulate:
- Which day is your highest volume day
- Exact loads used (via RPE)
- Whether deload comes at week 4 or 5
- Exercise substitutions for joint health

## Key Quotes

> "Auto-regulating your training (developing structured flexibility in your approach) can be very useful so that your training stress matches your ability to manage stress."
> — Helms, Training Pyramid, Level 1

> — Helms, Training Pyramid, Level 1
