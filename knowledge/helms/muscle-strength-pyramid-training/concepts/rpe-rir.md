# RPE / Repetitions in Reserve (RIR)

**Type:** concept
**Status:** solid
**Last Updated:** 2026-02-03
**Aliases:** Rating of Perceived Exertion, RIR, Repetitions Remaining, RPE Scale

## Summary

RPE (Rating of Perceived Exertion) based on Repetitions in Reserve (RIR) is a method for measuring intensity of effort. Popularized for powerlifting by Mike Tuchscherer and researched extensively by Dr. Michael Zourdos with Eric Helms, this system allows load prescription that adapts to daily readiness and individual variation in rep capacity at given percentages.

## The RPE Scale

| RPE | Meaning | RIR |
|-----|---------|-----|
| 10 | Could not do more reps or load | 0 |
| 9.5 | Could not do more reps, could do slightly more load | 0+ |
| 9 | Could do 1 more repetition | 1 |
| 8.5 | Could definitely do 1 more rep, chance at 2 | 1-2 |
| 8 | Could do 2 more repetitions | 2 |
| 7.5 | Could definitely do 2 more reps, chance at 3 | 2-3 |
| 7 | Could do 3 more repetitions | 3 |
| 5-6 | Could do 4-6 more repetitions | 4-6 |
| 1-4 | Very light to light effort | 6+ |

## Why RPE > Percentage of 1RM

**Problem with % 1RM:**
> "Depending on the person... one person may be able to do a very different number of repetitions at a similar percentage of 1RM compared to another. In fact in one study, among a group of resistance-trained males, there was a spread of 9 to 26 repetitions completed when performing an AMRAP with 70% 1RM on a back squat."
> — Helms, Training Pyramid, Level 2

**RPE Solution:**
If you prescribe 3×8×70% @ 6-8 RPE:
- "Bad at reps" lifter at 9 RPE on set 1 → reduces load for sets 2-3
- "Good at reps" lifter at 4 RPE on set 1 → increases load for sets 2-3
- Both end up at appropriate stimulus

## Combining RPE with Other Methods

**RPE + % 1RM:**
- Use % 1RM as starting point
- Use RPE to adjust
- Example: "3×5×80% @ 7-8 RPE"

**RPE + RM:**
- Use RM to set baseline
- RPE indicates when RM estimate needs updating
- A 6RM that feels like RPE 8 means you've gotten stronger

## Improving RPE Accuracy

Helms' recommendations for inexperienced lifters:
1. Record video and rate RPE after watching replay
2. Do a block with frequent failure training to learn true limits
3. Have experienced training partner rate your RPE
4. Hire a coach who uses RPE and get feedback on videos
5. Do set to target RPE, then go to failure to check accuracy

## RPE for Strength Testing

Can estimate 1RM without going to failure:
- Single at 7 RPE = estimated 4RM (3 reps in reserve)
- 2 reps at 8 RPE = estimated 4RM
- 2 reps at 9 RPE = estimated 3RM

> "Advanced lifters, especially those familiarized with using RPE in training, are quite accurate at gauging repetitions in reserve at the completion of a set when they are near failure."
> — Helms, Training Pyramid, Level 3

## Practical Guidelines

### Recommended RPE by Context

| Context | RPE Range | Rationale |
|---------|-----------|-----------|
| Volume/accumulation phase | 5-8 | High volume, manage fatigue |
| Intensity phase | 8-10 | Low volume, push limits |
| Deload | 5-7 | Recovery focus |
| Technique work | 5-7 | Quality over intensity |
| Strength testing | 9-10 | Express fitness |

### Failure Considerations

> "We don't want to perform the big, multi-joint compound lifts to muscular failure as the risk of injury is too high."
> — Helms, Training Pyramid, Level 2

- Isolation exercises: Safe to approach 10 RPE regularly
- Compound free weights: Keep at 9 RPE or below mostly
- Machine compounds: Can push closer to 10 RPE

## Key Quotes

> "Experienced lifters are very accurate when estimating RIR."
> — Helms, Training Pyramid, Level 2

> "Failure needs to be used with a purpose if used at all."
> — Helms, Training Pyramid, Level 2
