# Specificity (SAID Principle)

**Type:** concept
**Status:** solid
**Last Updated:** 2026-02-03
**Aliases:** SAID Principle, Specific Adaptations to Imposed Demands, Principle of Specificity

## Summary

The Principle of Specificity states that adaptations are specific to the demands imposed. To get stronger in a movement, train that movement. To develop 1RM strength, train with heavy loads. Specificity applies to movement patterns, load/rep ranges, and velocity. However, hypertrophy is notably less specific—muscle grows from various exercises and rep ranges, making it a general rather than specific adaptation.

## Core Principle

> "To get a certain outcome, we need to train for it."
> — Helms, Training Pyramid, Level 2

**SAID = Specific Adaptations to Imposed Demands**

## Types of Specificity

### Movement Specificity
- Squat strength transfers best to squatting
- Leg press strength transfers less to squat
- Even with equal muscle mass, unfamiliar movements show less strength

> "Simply put, when you do a lot of squats, you will improve your skill with the squat, develop the specific muscle groups that are activated during the squat, and the strength that you gain will be best expressed in the squat itself."
> — Helms, Training Pyramid, Level 4

### Load/Rep Range Specificity
- Strength is specific to the rep range trained
- 3RM training produces better 1RM gains than 10RM training (Schoenfeld, 2016)
- Training at 90%+ 1RM is more specific to 1RM testing than 80%

### Velocity Specificity
- Power training = light loads, high velocity
- Strength training = heavy loads, moderate velocity
- Sport-specific training matches competition demands

## Hypertrophy: The Exception

> "Hypertrophy does not require the same degree of specificity in terms of loading. Muscle can grow quite well with both moderate and heavy loading."
> — Helms, Training Pyramid, Level 2

**Why hypertrophy is less specific:**
- Survival adaptation (not a skill)
- Occurs from adequate volume at adequate effort
- Various exercises stimulate same muscles
- Various rep ranges produce similar growth

Research (Schoenfeld, 2014):
- 3RM vs 10RM groups, matched volume
- Equal muscle growth
- 3RM group gained more strength (specificity)

## Three Factors of Strength

1. **Muscle Mass** (and structural adaptations)
   - Larger cross-section = more fibers to recruit
   - Non-specific—any training that builds mass helps

2. **Neuromuscular Adaptations**
   - Motor unit recruitment
   - Rate coding
   - Specific to intensity/load trained

3. **Motor Patterns / Skill**
   - Movement proficiency
   - Highly specific to exercise practiced
   - "Strength is not just a quality of the body but also a skill"

## Extreme Specificity: Does It Work?

Bulgarian Method (daily 1RM attempts):
- Can work—case studies show improvement
- But may not be optimal

Gonzalez-Badillo study on intensity distribution:
- Three groups: 46, 93, or 184 reps at 90-100% 1RM
- Middle group (93 reps at 90%+) improved most
- Too much high-intensity work = diminishing returns

**Problems with extreme specificity:**
- High fatigue cost per rep
- More joint stress
- Mental burnout
- May not ingrain best technique if form degrades

## Practical Implications

### For Strength Athletes
- Competition lifts essential (high specificity)
- But not 100% of training
- Assistance work builds muscle (general adaptation)
- Recommendation: 50-75% competition lifts, 25-50% accessories

### For Hypertrophy
- Exercise selection less critical
- Many paths to same muscle growth
- Choose exercises based on:
  - Comfort and injury risk
  - Individual biomechanics
  - Weak point targeting
  - Enjoyment

## Key Quotes

> "Strength is specific to a movement and a rep range, so the more time you get moving loads with a specific exercise and a specific rep range, the stronger you will get on that specific exercise, in that specific rep range."
> — Helms, Training Pyramid, Level 2

> "The purpose of the human body is survival, so the adaptations it makes in response to a stress are always related back to being able to better handle that stress."
> — Helms, Training Pyramid, Level 2
