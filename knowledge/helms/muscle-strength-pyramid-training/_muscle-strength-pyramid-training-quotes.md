# Quotable Quotes from The Muscle and Strength Pyramid: Training

**Author:** Eric Helms, Andrea Valdez, Andy Morgan
**Last Updated:** 2026-02-03

## On Adherence

> "It doesn't matter how good your programming is if you cannot stick to it."

> "When you set up a training program there is nothing impressive about choosing something that cannot be sustained."

> "Optimal does not equal sustainable — don't set yourself up for failure."

> "Remember that 'optimal' is not necessarily the same thing as 'realistic'. Always think about fitting your training program to what is sustainable and realistic in your life first, before assessing anything else."

> "If you think that a 5 day a week training program would be perfect but you only have time to train 4 days a week — it's not an option, let go of it!"

> "Remember, consistency is what makes progress rather than perfection."

> "I guarantee you that if you take a suboptimal plan that you love, you'll put more effort into it than if you take an optimal plan that doesn't get your juices and your passion flowing."

## On the Big Picture

> "If you fail to plan, you plan to fail."

> "These are principles and guidelines, not rules."

> "There isn't a right or wrong way to train. We're just trying to get you in a ballpark of the right place to start so that you can adjust."

> "Progress is not an on or off switch. It's not that we make gains or we don't, it's maybe we make better progress by adjusting some of the variables."

> "The question is, what do you do about it when it happens?"

## On Volume and Intensity

> "Do enough to progress, not as much as possible. Increase when plateaued if you are recovering well."

> "Volume, intensity and frequency are what actually form the foundation of programs."

> "Too much volume can have negative impacts on both hypertrophy and strength."

> "Just do enough volume to progress and only increase when you need, not want, to do more."

> "10–20 sets per muscle group/movement, a place to start and adjust from, not a law to live your life by."

## On Fatigue and Recovery

> "Fatigue masks fitness and therefore affects performance."

> "Fatigue dissipates at a faster rate than fitness, which is why deloads can be such useful tools."

> "No plan survives contact with the enemy."

> "The reality is that vacations, injuries, travel, sports you might play recreationally or competitively, and unexpected work commitments are not the problem. Rather, the stress, indecision, overreaction and emotional decisions made in response to these minor hiccups are."

## On Training to Failure

> "Failure needs to be used with a purpose if used at all."

> "We don't want to perform the big, multi-joint compound lifts to muscular failure as the risk of injury is too high."

## On Periodization

> "Periodization is often overcomplicated and misunderstood. Simply put, periodization is the intentional manipulation of variables over time to achieve specific goals."

> "Asking 'Which type of periodization is the best?' is the wrong question."

> "Almost every program is linear to some degree. Additionally, very rarely are you doing the same exact rep range every day or week, for months on end, so almost every program undulates."

> "Don't think in black and white when considering periodization."

## On Progressive Overload

> "In order to keep getting bigger and stronger you need to gradually increase the training stimulus. This is called 'The Principle of Progressive Overload.'"

> "If you are gaining strength on most of your movements, that's a good sign that you are providing adequate overload."

> "It's easy to get hung up on strength gains... But don't get so hung up on strength to the point that you think putting weight on the bar is the only thing that matters, or that doing so is what causes hypertrophy. It's actually the other way around."

## On Specificity

> "To get a certain outcome, we need to train for it."

> "Strength is not just a quality of the body but also a skill."

> "Simply put, when you do a lot of squats, you will improve your skill with the squat, develop the specific muscle groups that are activated during the squat, and the strength that you gain will be best expressed in the squat itself."

## On Training Age and Progress

> "Nobody can get bigger and stronger forever and everyone has a genetic limit. In general, the closer we are to our limit; the slower progress will be."

> "Not everyone can be a world-class performer, but everyone can push themselves nearer to the limits of their own talents."

> "I'm not an elite powerlifter and I'm not 'advanced' by that standard, almost certainly never will be, but this doesn't mean I'm done improving."

## On Competition

> "A great training plan and taper can be ruined by poor attempt selection on meet day."

> "Be smart, build the biggest total you can rather than focusing on individual lift personal bests."

## On Research and Black-and-White Thinking

> "Don't look at the volume, intensity and frequency guidelines with your absolutist glasses on."

> "We don't really know what optimal is. There is no way to know."

> "Don't fall into the trap of constantly questioning and changing what you are doing in the pursuit of the magic 'optimal' formula."

## On Life Balance

> "Our coaching service is called '3D Muscle Journey', not '3D Muscle Tomorrow'."

> "Life doesn't stop for lifting."

> "I would argue that being a happy, well-rounded person might be even more optimal if you look at it globally (which you always should)."
