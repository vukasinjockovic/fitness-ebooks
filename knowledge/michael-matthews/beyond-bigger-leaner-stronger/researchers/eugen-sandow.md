## Summary

Eugen Sandow (1867-1925) was a Prussian-born strongman who is considered the "father of bodybuilding." Known for his resemblance to classical Greek and Roman sculptures, he systematically measured ancient statues to derive the "Grecian Ideal" proportions—the formula for building an aesthetic physique.

## Key Contribution

### The Grecian Ideal

> "Eugen Sandow, the legendary strongman from the late 1800s and 'father of bodybuilding,' was renowned for his resemblance to the classical Greek and Roman sculptures. It was no accident."
— Ch. 3

> "Sandow measured the statues in museums and found that certain proportions remained constant (and as you now know, these proportions have their roots in the Golden Ratio). This led to the development of the 'Grecian Ideal' as a formula for building the perfect physique, and Sandow's goal was to embody it."
— Ch. 3

## Influence on Bodybuilding

> "Sandow's body and principles served as the model for future bodybuilders who became known for their beautifully balanced physiques, like Steve Reeves, Frank Zane, Danny Padilla, Serge Nubret, Bob Paris, and Arnold Schwarzenegger."
— Ch. 3

## Historical Significance

Sandow brought together:
1. Ancient Greek ideals of physical beauty
2. The Golden Ratio mathematics
3. Systematic measurement and training
4. Showmanship and public performance

His approach transformed physical culture from strongman feats into aesthetic bodybuilding.
