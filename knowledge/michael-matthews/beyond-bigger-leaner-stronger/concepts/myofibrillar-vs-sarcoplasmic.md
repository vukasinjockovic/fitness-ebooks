# Myofibrillar vs Sarcoplasmic Hypertrophy

**Type:** concept
**Status:** solid
**Last Updated:** 2026-02-04
**Aliases:** Two Types of Muscle Growth, Fiber vs Fluid Growth

## Summary

The two types of muscle hypertrophy: myofibrillar (actual fiber growth) and sarcoplasmic (fluid/glycogen volume increase). Understanding this distinction explains why some muscles look "hard and dense" while others look "puffy" and deflate quickly—and why heavy lifting should be prioritized.

## Definitions

### Myofibrillar Hypertrophy

> "Myofibrillar hypertrophy refers to an actual increase in size of the muscle fibers."
— Ch. 5

**Characteristics:**
- Actual growth of contractile proteins
- Increases strength proportionally
- Dense, "hard" looking muscle
- Doesn't deflate significantly with rest
- Primary from heavy lifting (80-95% 1RM)

### Sarcoplasmic Hypertrophy

> "Sarcoplasmic hypertrophy [refers] to an increase in the volume of the fluid, non-contractile components of the muscle (glycogen, water, minerals, etc.)."
— Ch. 5

**Characteristics:**
- Increases muscle volume without proportional strength gain
- "Puffy" looking muscle
- Deflates noticeably with rest/detraining
- Primary from higher rep lifting (10-12+)

## The Rep Range Continuum

> "If we look at rep ranges on a continuum going from very heavy weight for one rep to very light weights for 40+ reps, the following generally holds true: Heavier lifting preferentially induces myofibrillar growth, and lighter lifting sarcoplasmic growth."
— Ch. 5

| Rep Range | Primary Adaptation |
|-----------|-------------------|
| 1-3 | Neurological (pure strength) |
| 4-6 | Myofibrillar + strength |
| 6-8 | Mixed (more myofibrillar) |
| 8-12 | Mixed (more sarcoplasmic) |
| 12+ | Sarcoplasmic + endurance |

## The Problem with High-Rep Only Training

> "This too sheds some light on another big downside to working exclusively in the 10 to 12 rep range and up: you're inducing more sarcoplasmic growth than myofibrillar, and the result is that puffy type of muscle that noticeably deflates after several days of rest."
— Ch. 5

Matthews' personal experience:
> "I used to train in the 10 to 12 rep range with a lot of supersets, drop sets, and giant sets, and I always hated the inflation-deflation cycle. I would train my chest on Monday and look good for a few days but then wonder where it went come Saturday."
— Ch. 5

## Benefits of Heavy Lifting Foundation

> "I now have several years of heavy (myofibrillar) lifting under my belt and have built a foundation of strong, dense muscle that just doesn't deflate. I can take 1 or 2 weeks off from training and lose maybe 5% of my visual size, and I retain strength surprisingly well."
— Ch. 5

## Sarcoplasmic Has Its Place

> "Don't think that sarcoplasmic growth is worthless. It has a place in an advanced weightlifter's routine, but it should not be the focus."
— Ch. 5

In BBLS, sarcoplasmic sets (8-10 reps) are included for:
- Metabolic stress pathway
- Muscle endurance
- Additional volume
- Type IIa fiber stimulation

But they comprise only 2 of ~10 working sets per workout.

## Why BLS First, Then BBLS

> "Focusing on sarcoplasmic growth fails to build any real strength, so you stay relatively weak, even if you have some size. Focusing on myofibrillar growth, on the other hand, can greatly increase your strength."
— Ch. 5

Beginners need 1-2 years of BLS to build myofibrillar foundation before adding sarcoplasmic work.
