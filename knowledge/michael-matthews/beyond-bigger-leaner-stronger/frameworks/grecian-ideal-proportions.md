# Grecian Ideal Proportions

**Type:** framework
**Status:** solid
**Last Updated:** 2026-02-04
**Aliases:** Golden Ratio Physique, Ideal Body Measurements

## Summary

A mathematical framework for building an aesthetically pleasing physique based on the Golden Ratio (1:1.618). Traced back to ancient Greek statues and systematized by Eugen Sandow, who measured classical sculptures to derive the "ideal" proportions.

## The Golden Ratio

> "Two quantities are in the Golden Ratio if the ratio of the sum of the quantities to the larger quantity is equal to the ratio of the larger quantity to the smaller one."
— Ch. 3

Numerically: **1:1.618**

> "Nowhere is the Golden Ratio more exemplified than in the human body, however, as da Vinci knew so long ago. In fact, he found that the more the body reflected this proportion, the more beautiful it was."
— Ch. 3

## Historical Background

### Marcus Vitruvius (1st Century BC)
- Roman architect who described ideal human proportions
- Believed the human body reflected "hidden geometry of the universe"

### Leonardo da Vinci (1487)
- Drew the Vitruvian Man based on Vitruvius's observations
- "Man is a model of the world"

### Eugen Sandow (Late 1800s)

> "Sandow measured the statues in museums and found that certain proportions remained constant (and as you now know, these proportions have their roots in the Golden Ratio). This led to the development of the 'Grecian Ideal' as a formula for building the perfect physique."
— Ch. 3

## The Formulas

### Arms
> "Your flexed arms should be 150% larger than the circumference of your non-dominant wrist (wrist measurement x 2.5)"
— Ch. 3

Example: 7" wrist → 17.5" flexed arms

### Calves
> "Your flexed calves should match your flexed arms"
— Ch. 3

### Shoulders
> "Your shoulder circumference should measure 1.618 times larger than your waist (waist x 1.618)"
— Ch. 3

Example: 32" waist → 51.8" shoulders

### Chest
> "Your chest circumference should be 550% larger than the circumference of your non-dominant wrist (wrist measurement x 6.5)"
— Ch. 3

Example: 7" wrist → 45.5" chest

### Upper Legs
> "Your upper leg circumference should be 75% larger than your knee circumference (knee measurement x 1.75)"
— Ch. 3

## Measurement Guidelines

| Body Part | How to Measure |
|-----------|----------------|
| Wrist | Smallest part, non-dominant hand |
| Arm | Largest part of flexed bicep/tricep |
| Calf | Largest part while flexed (heel raised) |
| Waist | Natural waistline (above belly button, below ribs) |
| Shoulders | Around shoulders/chest at widest point, arms at sides |
| Chest | Fullest part of pecs, under armpits, across shoulder blades |
| Upper leg | Largest part of thigh/hamstring |
| Knee | At tip of kneecap |

## Important Caveat

> "If you're carrying excess fat, your measurements will be skewed... If you want to truly know what needs improving, you need to get lean first."
— Ch. 3

Recommended: Measure at 10% body fat or lower (Matthews prefers 7-8%).

## Using the Proportions

> "Building an aesthetic physique is formulaic, and anyone can do it."
— Ch. 3

1. Get lean (≤10% body fat)
2. Take all measurements
3. Compare to ideal ratios
4. Identify weak points
5. Program additional volume for lagging areas
