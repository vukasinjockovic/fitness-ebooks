# Carb Cycling Protocol

**Type:** framework
**Status:** solid
**Last Updated:** 2026-02-04
**Aliases:** Carbohydrate Cycling, High/Low Carb Days

## Summary

Matthews provides a skeptical but nuanced review of carb cycling. While he debunks many marketing claims about metabolic advantages, he acknowledges two specific use cases where carb cycling may help: getting very lean (<7% body fat) and maintaining extremely low body fat.

## What Is Carb Cycling?

> "For example, you may do 4 low-carb days, followed by a high-carb day, and then a no-carb day, and then start over. Or you may do 3 low-carb days followed by 1 high-carb day."
— Ch. 21

## Typical Protocol Structure

### High-Carb Day
> "A high-carb day will generally have you eating 2 to 2.5 grams of carbohydrate per pound of body weight. Your protein intake will be around 1 gram per pound, and your fat intake will be as low as possible."
— Ch. 21

### Low-Carb Day
> "A low-carb day will call for about 0.5 grams of carbohydrate per pound of body weight. Your protein intake will usually increase to about 1.5 grams per pound, and your fat intake will increase to around 0.35 grams per pound."
— Ch. 21

| Day Type | Carbs | Protein | Fat |
|----------|-------|---------|-----|
| High | 2-2.5 g/lb | 1 g/lb | Minimal |
| Low | 0.5 g/lb | 1.5 g/lb | 0.35 g/lb |
| No | Near zero | High | Moderate |

## Marketing Claims vs Reality

### Claim: Don't Need to Count Calories
> "Part of the appeal of carb cycling is the claim that you don't have to count calories or watch what you eat."
— Ch. 21

**Reality:** Still need overall caloric deficit for fat loss.

### Claim: Low-Carb Days Accelerate Fat Loss
> "The theory that low-carb days deliver the big fat loss punch of carb cycling are not supported by literature. They are simply part of the marketing pitch."
— Ch. 21

### Claim: Better Hunger Control
Research shows:
> "Hunger ratings improved for both diets with no difference between them."
— Ch. 21

### Claim: High-Carb Days Boost Metabolism
The supposed "metabolic spike" from high-carb days is overstated.

## The Real Downsides

### Training Suffers

> "Training on a no/low-carb diet is even worse, and one or two higher carb days are not enough to offset this. If you want to drag ass and have basically the worst workouts ever, try to lift with any intensity following a couple of no/low-carb days."
— Ch. 21

### Psychological Stress

> "Most people get very stressed out on their no/low-carb days. If you want to know what carb cravings are like, eat fewer than 50 grams of carbs per day for a few days."
— Ch. 21

## When Carb Cycling May Help

Matthews identifies two legitimate use cases:

### 1. Getting Very Lean (<7% BF)

> "If you're looking to get really lean (under 7% body fat), then carb cycling may be able to help. While it may or may not inherently speed up fat loss, it can make maintaining a weekly calorie deficit easier for some."
— Ch. 21

### 2. Maintaining Very Lean (<7% BF)

> "If you're looking to stay really lean (7% or under), carb cycling may also be able to help. Many bodybuilders and fitness competitors use carb cycling to stay in contest shape for a couple of months out of the year."
— Ch. 21

## The Logic for Contest Prep

> "When you carb cycle, some days are higher calorie than others, but the weekly deficit is ultimately what matters. Some people enjoy eating less on their off days (they usually make these their low/no-carb days) and then being able to eat more on their training days."
— Ch. 21

## Matthews' Verdict

For most people: **Not worth it.**

The complexity, training interference, and psychological stress don't provide meaningful advantages over simpler approaches for people above 7% body fat.
