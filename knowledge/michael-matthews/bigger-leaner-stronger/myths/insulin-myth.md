# The Insulin Myth

**Type:** concept
**Status:** solid
**Last Updated:** 2026-02-04
**Aliases:** Carbs Make You Fat, Insulin Hypothesis

## Summary

Matthews devotes significant space to debunking the popular belief that insulin (and by extension, carbohydrates) makes you fat. He argues this is a perversion of basic physiology used to create a "bogeyman that doesn't exist."

## The Myth

The flawed logic chain:
> "High-carb diet = high insulin levels = burn less fat and store more = get fatter and fatter."
— Ch. 12

And its corollary:
> "Low-carb diet = low insulin levels = burn more fat and store less = stay lean."
— Ch. 12

## The Reality

### Insulin's Actual Function

> "When you eat food, your pancreas releases insulin into the blood, and its job is to shuttle the nutrients from the food into cells for use."
— Ch. 12

### The Partial Truth

> "The answer relates to one of insulin's functions that influences fat storage. Specifically, it inhibits the metabolic breakdown of fat cells for energy and stimulates the creation of body fat. That is, insulin tells the body to stop burning its fat stores and instead absorb some of the fatty acids and glucose in the blood and turn them into more body fat."
— Ch. 12

### The Full Picture

> "While it's true that insulin causes fat cells to absorb fatty acids and glucose and thus expand, that physiological mechanism isn't what causes you to get fatter over time—overeating does."
— Ch. 12

### Even Without Carbs

> "Another little fact that those with a phobia of carbs and insulin like to ignore is that the body doesn't need high levels of insulin to store dietary fat as body fat thanks to an enzyme called acylation stimulating protein."
— Ch. 12

## Research Evidence

### When Calories Are Matched

> "There is no significant difference in weight loss."
— Ch. 12, citing 4 studies comparing high-carb vs low-carb diets with matched protein

### Mixed Meals Mitigate Response

> "Furthermore, when you mix carbohydrates (all forms) with other forms, the insulin response is mitigated. That is, eating a couple of tablespoons of sucrose on an empty stomach causes a larger insulin reaction in the body than eating a couple of tablespoons of sucrose as a part of a mixed meal."
— Ch. 12

## Why the Myth Persists

1. **Correlation confusion** - High-carb diets often mean high-calorie diets
2. **Marketing** - Low-carb diets are profitable industries
3. **Partial science** - Real mechanism taken out of context
4. **Initial water loss** - Low-carb causes rapid scale drop (water, not fat)

## The Fundamental Law

> "Contrary to (currently) popular belief, it doesn't matter how many carbohydrates you eat or how high your insulin levels are throughout the day. Energy balance is the first law of thermodynamics at work: fat stores can't be increased without the provision of excess energy, nor can they be reduced without the restriction of energy."
— Ch. 7

## Insulin's Benefits

### Anti-Catabolic Effect

> "When insulin levels are elevated, the rate at which muscle proteins are broken down decreases. This, in turn, creates a more anabolic environment in which muscles can grow larger more quickly."
— Ch. 12

This is why Matthews recommends carbs, especially around workouts.

## Key Quotes

> "This scientifically debunked myth is little more than the perversion of basic physiology to convince you of a bogeyman that doesn't exist."
— Ch. 12

> "Why is insulin so viciously attacked by mainstream diet 'gurus,' though? It plays a vital physiological role in our bodies—a role that we simply couldn't live without. Why are we told that it makes us fat and sick?"
— Ch. 12
