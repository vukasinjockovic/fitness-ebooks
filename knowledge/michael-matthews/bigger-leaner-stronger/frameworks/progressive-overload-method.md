# Progressive Overload Method

**Type:** framework
**Status:** solid
**Last Updated:** 2026-02-04
**Aliases:** BLS Progression System, Double Progression

## Summary

The specific progression protocol used in Bigger Leaner Stronger: when you achieve 6 reps on any set, add 10 pounds (5 per side) for subsequent sets. This "double progression" method (progressing on reps, then weight) ensures continuous progressive overload—the primary driver of muscle growth.

## The Protocol

### Basic Rule

> "Once you hit 6 reps for one set, you add weight for your next set."
— Ch. 16

### Weight Increase

| Equipment | Increase |
|-----------|----------|
| Barbell | 10 lbs total (5 per side) |
| Dumbbell | 5 lbs per hand |

### Example Application

**Incline Bench Press:**
1. Set 1: 225 lbs × 6 reps → **Add weight**
2. Set 2: 235 lbs × 4 reps
3. Set 3: 235 lbs × 4 reps

### If Jump Is Too Big

If you only get 2-3 reps after adding weight:
- Option A: Reduce by 5 lbs (use 2.5 lb plates)
- Option B: Drop back to previous weight, finish remaining sets
- Next week: Start fresh, attempt full jump again

## Why This Works

### Progressive Tension Overload

> "The answer is known as progressive tension overload, which means progressively increasing tension levels in the muscle fibers over time. That is, lifting progressively heavier and heavier weights."
— Ch. 6

### Adaptation Signal

> "Muscles must be given a powerful reason to grow, and nothing is more convincing than subjecting them to more and more mechanical stress and tension."
— Ch. 6

### Research Support

Arizona State University meta-analysis of 140 studies:
> "A progression in resistance optimizes strength gains and muscle growth... working in the 4- to 6-rep range (80 percent of one-rep max) is most effective for those who train regularly."
— Ch. 6

## Weekly Goals

> "Your primary goal with every workout should be beating the previous week's numbers, even if only by 1 rep."
— Ch. 16

### Acceptable Progress

- Adding 1 rep to any set = progress
- Maintaining all weights/reps = acceptable (plateau)
- Slight strength dip = normal during cuts

### Unacceptable Patterns

- Multiple weeks of no progress → assess recovery/diet
- Dramatic strength loss → check calorie intake
- Chronic stalling → consider deload

## Reality Check

> "Some weeks just won't go like that. Sometimes you'll only be able to lift exactly what you did the week prior. Sometimes you'll even be a rep weaker."
— Ch. 16

> "These things happen and don't necessarily mean anything is wrong. Just keep working at it and you should see a slow, steady increase in weight lifted over time."
— Ch. 16

## Comparison to Other Methods

| Method | Description | BLS Approach |
|--------|-------------|--------------|
| Linear progression | Add weight every session | More sustainable: add when hitting 6 |
| Percentage-based | Work at % of 1RM | Rep-based: work to 4-6 reps |
| RPE-based | Train to perceived effort | Objective: hit rep target |
| Volume progression | Add sets over time | Fixed sets: progress on weight |

## Long-Term Expectations

Matthews' personal results after switching to this method:
- 20 lbs of additional muscle
- Doubled weights on all major lifts
- Went from 197 lbs at 16% BF to 187 lbs at 7% BF
