# HIIT Cardio Protocol

**Type:** framework
**Status:** solid
**Last Updated:** 2026-02-04
**Aliases:** High-Intensity Interval Training, Interval Cardio

## Summary

Matthews recommends high-intensity interval training (HIIT) over steady-state cardio for fat loss. HIIT burns more calories in less time, preserves muscle better, and provides greater metabolic benefits through the "afterburn effect."

## Why HIIT Over Steady-State

### More Fat Loss, Less Time

> "HIIT not only burns more fat in less time than steady-state cardio, but it preserves muscular size and performance better as well."
— Ch. 19

Example cited:
> "After six weeks of training, the subjects doing the intervals had lost significantly more body fat. Yes, four to six 30-second sprints burn more fat than 60 minutes of incline treadmill walking."
— Ch. 19

### Muscle Preservation

> "Research has shown that the longer your cardio sessions are, the more they impair strength and hypertrophy. Thus, keeping your cardio sessions short is important when we're talking about maximizing your gains in the weight room and preserving your muscle."
— Ch. 19

### The Afterburn Effect

> "Cardio, especially a variety I recommend called high-intensity interval cardio (HIIT), can increase your basal metabolic rate through what's known as the 'afterburn effect.' While that sounds fancy and is often used in sketchy marketing pitches for sketchy products, it's simple: your body continues burning additional energy after you exercise."
— Ch. 7

## The Protocol

### Matthews' Personal Method (Recumbent Bike)

1. Start with 2-3 minutes of low-intensity warm-up
2. Pedal at maximum effort/resistance for 30 seconds
3. Reduce resistance to slowest setting, pedal moderately for 45-60 seconds
4. Repeat cycle for 25-30 minutes total

### Beginner Modification

> "If you're new to HIIT, you may need to extend this rest period to 90 to 120 seconds."
— Ch. 19

### Alternative Modalities

> "If you'd like to do a different form of HIIT cardio, such as rowing, sprinting, swimming, jump roping, or anything else that permits it, go for it. You can apply the same simple principles: relatively short bursts of maximum effort that spike your heart rate followed by low-intensity recovery periods that bring it down to normal levels."
— Ch. 19

## Frequency Recommendations

| Goal | HIIT Sessions/Week |
|------|-------------------|
| Bulking | 2 × 25 minutes |
| Cutting | 3-5 × 25 minutes |
| Maintaining | 2-3 × 25 minutes |

## Timing Considerations

> "I recommend that you separate your weightlifting and cardio sessions by at least a few hours if at all possible."
— Ch. 19

> "If there's no way that you can split up your cardio and weightlifting, do your weight training first, as cardio first will drain energy that you'll want for your lifting."
— Ch. 19

## Benefits Summary

From Ch. 19:
- Improved insulin sensitivity in muscles
- Greater post-exercise calorie burn
- Better muscle preservation
- Time efficiency (25-30 min vs 60+ min)
- Improved cardiovascular fitness

## Cardio Reality Check

> "Many people are shocked to learn that I do no more than 1.5 to 2 hours of cardio per week while cutting but am able to get to the 6 to 7 percent body fat range with ease. Well, the idea that you have to do a ton of cardio to get shredded is a complete myth."
— Ch. 19

## Warning on Excess

> "If you'd like to stick with steady-state cardio or include it in your routine, stick with the frequency recommendations given above. You can mix and match modalities (HIIT vs low-intensity steady-state, or LISS) but I still wouldn't do more than five sessions per week."
— Ch. 19

## Key Quotes

> "Only high-intensity interval training allows you to [keep cardio short] and burn enough fat to make it worthwhile."
— Ch. 19
