# Energy Balance

**Type:** concept
**Status:** solid
**Last Updated:** 2026-02-04
**Aliases:** Calories In/Calories Out, CICO, Thermodynamics of Weight Loss

## Summary

The foundational principle underlying all weight change: your body stores fat when you consume more energy than you burn and loses fat when you consume less than you burn. Matthews emphasizes this is non-negotiable physics, regardless of food choices, meal timing, or macronutrient ratios.

## Key Findings

### The First Law of Fat Loss

> "You simply can't get fatter unless you feed your body more energy than it burns, and you can't get leaner unless you feed it less energy than it burns."
— Ch. 7

### Fed vs Fasted States

The body cycles between:
- **Fed state (postprandial):** Storing nutrients as fat
- **Fasted state (postabsorptive):** Burning fat for energy

> "Your body flips between 'fed' and 'fasted' states every day, storing fat from the food you eat and then burning it once there's nothing left to use from the meals."
— Ch. 7

### The Math

| Condition | Result |
|-----------|--------|
| Calories in = Calories out | Weight stable |
| Calories in > Calories out | Fat gain |
| Calories in < Calories out | Fat loss |

## What It Illustrates

### Professor Mark Haub's "Twinkie Diet"

Referenced experiment from Kansas State University:
- Started: 211 lbs, 33.4% body fat
- Ate: ~1,800 calories/day
- Food: 2/3 from Twinkies, Little Debbies, Doritos, Oreos
- Result: Lost 27 pounds in two months
- Bonus: LDL dropped 20%, HDL increased 20%

> "When it comes to fat loss, calories are king."
— Ch. 8

### Insulin Myth Debunked

> "Contrary to (currently) popular belief, it doesn't matter how many carbohydrates you eat or how high your insulin levels are throughout the day. Energy balance is the first law of thermodynamics at work."
— Ch. 7

### Low-Carb vs High-Carb

When protein is matched and calories equal:
> "There is no significant difference in weight loss."
— Ch. 12, citing 4 studies

## Nuances

### A Calorie Is NOT Always a Calorie

For body composition (not just weight):

> "A calorie is a calorie for weight-loss purposes alone, but a calorie is not a calorie when it comes to optimizing body composition."
— Ch. 7

Why macros matter:
- Too little protein → muscle loss
- Too few carbs → impaired performance, catabolic hormones
- Too little fat → hormone dysfunction

### Metabolic Adaptation

When chronically restricting calories:
> "Your body reduces its total fat stores to stay alive, but it also slows down its basal metabolic rate to conserve energy."
— Ch. 8

This is why crash diets fail long-term.

## Practical Applications

### For Fat Loss
1. Calculate TDEE
2. Create 20-25% deficit
3. Prioritize protein (1.2g/lb)
4. Track intake accurately
5. Adjust based on results

### Hidden Calorie Traps
> "Hidden calories are those that you don't realize are there and account for... by far the number-one reason why people fail to get results."
— Ch. 14

Examples:
- 2 tbsp olive oil: 240 cal
- 2 tbsp mayo: 200 cal
- Cream in coffee: 80 cal

## Key Quotes

> "If you store just as much fat as you burn every day, your weight stays the same. If you store more fat than you burn (by overeating), you get fatter. And if you burn more fat than you store, you get leaner."
— Ch. 7

> "It doesn't matter what you eat. If your metabolism is healthy and you set your calorie intake correctly—if you maintain a moderate calorie deficit by eating a bit less energy than you burn every day—you will lose weight."
— Ch. 8
