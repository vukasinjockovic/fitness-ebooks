# Effective Reps

**Type:** concept
**Status:** authoritative
**Last Updated:** 2026-02-03
**Aliases:** Effective Repetitions, Near-Failure Reps, Stimulative Reps

## Summary

Effective reps are the repetitions performed at 5 RIR or lower within a set—the last ~5 reps before muscular failure. These reps see the vast majority of tension produced by the biggest, most growth-prone motor units. This activation pattern occurs regardless of load within the stimulus range, making proximity to failure a key driver of hypertrophy stimulus.

## Why the Last 5 Reps Matter

### Motor Unit Recruitment Pattern

As a set approaches muscular failure:
1. Smaller, slow-twitch motor units begin to fatigue
2. Larger, fast-twitch motor units must activate to continue the movement
3. By 5 RIR, nearly all motor units including the most growth-prone are engaged
4. This pattern holds true regardless of load (within 30-85% 1RM range)

### Evidence for the 5 RIR Threshold

> "The last five or so reps of a set taken close to failure sees the vast majority of the tension produced by the biggest and most growth-prone motor units."

> "This activation occurs in the last five reps approaching failure no matter the load within the stimulus range."

## Relationship Between RIR and Growth

Growth increases as RIR decreases, but with diminishing returns:

| RIR Transition | Growth Increase |
|----------------|-----------------|
| 5 → 4 RIR | Large jump |
| 4 → 3 RIR | Notable increase |
| 3 → 2 RIR | Moderate increase |
| 2 → 1 RIR | Smaller increase |
| 1 → 0 RIR | Smallest increase |

The jump from 5 RIR to 4 RIR produces more additional growth than the jump from 1 RIR to 0 RIR.

## Practical Implications

### Load Independence

The effective reps concept explains how different rep ranges can produce similar hypertrophy:

- **5-rep set to failure**: All 5 reps are "effective reps"
- **30-rep set to failure**: Last ~5 reps are "effective reps"
- Both produce high motor unit recruitment in those final reps

### Volume Counting

Counting "hard sets" (sets at 5-0 RIR) is a valid proxy for growth stimulus:
- 2 sets at 2-3 RIR ≈ roughly 2x growth of 1 set at 0 RIR
- More sets at moderate RIR often beats fewer sets at 0 RIR

### Why Not Just Train at 0 RIR?

While 0 RIR maximizes per-set stimulus:
- Generates disproportionately more fatigue
- Limits progressive overload capability
- Increases injury risk
- Degrades technique

The **best stimulus-to-fatigue ratio** is achieved averaging 2-3 RIR across a program.

## Key Quotes

> "Reps of 5 RIR and lower have been termed 'effective reps.' To be clear, the technically correct way to envision them is as 'more effective reps,' because the reps before them are also stimulative, just less so."

> "The last five reps of a 30 rep set to failure might require almost as much activation as the last five in a five rep set taken to failure."
