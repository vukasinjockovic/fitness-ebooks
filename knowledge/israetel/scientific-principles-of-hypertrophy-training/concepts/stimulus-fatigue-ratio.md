# Stimulus to Fatigue Ratio (SFR)

**Type:** concept
**Status:** authoritative
**Last Updated:** 2026-02-03
**Aliases:** SFR, Stimulus-Fatigue Ratio, S:F Ratio

## Summary

The Stimulus to Fatigue Ratio (SFR) is a measure of how much muscle growth stimulus a training input provides relative to the fatigue it generates. It unites the three most important training principles: Specificity, Overload, and Fatigue Management. Higher SFR values indicate more efficient training—more growth stimulus for less fatigue cost.

## The Formula

```
SFR = Hypertrophy Stimulated ÷ Fatigue Generated

Or more formally:

SFR = RSM / Fatigue
    = (Mind-Muscle Connection + Pump + Muscle Disruption) /
      (Joint/CT Disruption + Perceived Exertion + Unused Muscle Performance Loss)
```

Each component is rated 0-3, giving:
- Numerator (RSM): 0-9
- Denominator (Fatigue): 0-9

## Components Explained

### Stimulus Components (RSM - Raw Stimulus Magnitude)

1. **Mind-Muscle Connection** (0-3)
   - 0: Barely aware of target muscles
   - 1: Target muscles worked mildly
   - 2: Good tension and/or burn
   - 3: Tension and burn close to the limit

2. **Pump** (0-3)
   - 0: No pump
   - 1: Very mild pump
   - 2: Decent pump
   - 3: Close to maximal pump

3. **Muscle Disruption** (0-3)
   - 0: No fatigue, perturbation, or soreness
   - 1: Some weakness/stiffness, recovered by next day
   - 2: Soreness lasting through following day
   - 3: Much weaker, DOMS for several days

### Fatigue Components

1. **Joint and Connective Tissue Disruption** (0-3)
   - 0: Minimal to no pain or perturbation
   - 1: Some pain, recovered by next day
   - 2: Persistent pain lasting several days
   - 3: Chronic pain persisting weeks or longer

2. **Perceived Exertion** (0-3)
   - 0: Very easy, hardly taxing
   - 1: Effort required, recovered by end of day
   - 2: Large effort, drained through next day
   - 3: All-out effort, drained for days

3. **Unused Muscle Performance** (0-3)
   - 0: Better than expected
   - 1: As expected
   - 2: Worse than expected
   - 3: Hugely deteriorated

## Improving SFR

**Higher SFR exercises allow for:**
- Application of high forces through largest ROMs
- Minimal involvement of non-target muscles
- Lowest possible energy expenditure
- Lowest axial loading

**Practical techniques:**
- Use full range of motion
- Focus on mind-muscle connection
- Control eccentric and concentric movements
- Avoid swinging/momentum
- Execute movement to target intended muscle (not just move weight)
- Execute to reduce wear and tear
- Individualize technique to your body

## SFR Relationships

### Volume and SFR
- SFR is highest at lowest volumes (per-set stimulus high, fatigue low)
- As volume increases, SFR decreases (more fatigue per additional set)
- BUT: More absolute growth requires higher volumes despite lower SFR

### RIR and SFR
- Very high RIR (>5): Low stimulus, requires more volume → lower SFR
- 4-1 RIR: Best stimulus-to-fatigue balance → highest SFR
- 0 RIR: High stimulus, very high fatigue → lower SFR

**Optimal Average:** 2-3 RIR likely maximizes SFR for most training

### Training Age and SFR
- Beginners: Best SFRs at lower volumes, higher RIR
- Advanced: Must train at lower RIR despite worse SFR
- Pro bodybuilder example: "Train nine times a week for hours to gain 5lbs per year"

## Exercise Ranking Indices

| Index | Formula | Use When |
|-------|---------|----------|
| **RSM** | Mind-Muscle + Pump + Disruption | Recovery not limiting, want max stimulus |
| **SFR** | RSM ÷ Fatigue | Recovery limited, want efficiency |
| **STR** | RSM ÷ Time | Time limited, want efficiency |
| **SFTR** | RSM ÷ (Fatigue × Time) | Both time and recovery limited |
