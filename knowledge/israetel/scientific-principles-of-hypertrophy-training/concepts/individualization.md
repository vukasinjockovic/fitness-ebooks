# Individualization

**Type:** concept
**Status:** authoritative
**Last Updated:** 2026-02-03
**Aliases:** Individual Differences, Personal Optimization, Autoregulation

## Summary

Individualization is the process by which training principles are tailored to an individual's unique needs and responses to stimuli. What constitutes optimal overload, volume, frequency, and recovery for one person may be excessive or insufficient for another. Effective individualization means finding specific responses to training variables and fine-tuning training accordingly.

## Core Principle

> "Individualization of the training principles almost always involves differences in the magnitude of their application or the specific type of application, not in whether principles need to be applied at all."

Key insight: Everyone needs Overload, Fatigue Management, etc.—individualization adjusts HOW MUCH, not WHETHER.

## Key Individual Variables

### Work Capacity

**Definition:** Ability to tolerate a given amount of work at a given load for a given duration.

**Example:**
- Person A: 135lb rows for 10,8,7,5,5 reps
- Person B (same 1RM): 135lb rows for 10,9,9,8,8 reps
- Person B has greater work capacity

**Programming implications:**
- Higher work capacity → can handle higher per-session volumes
- Lower work capacity → need more sessions to distribute volume
- Caveat: High work capacity often correlates with slower-twitch fibers

### Recovery Ability

**Definition:** Rate at which various systems recover from training stress.

**Varies between:**
- Different individuals (genetics, lifestyle, age)
- Different body systems in same person (neuromuscular vs connective tissue)
- Different circumstances (sleep, stress, nutrition)

**Programming implications:**
- Faster recovery → higher frequencies possible
- Slower recovery → more rest between sessions
- System-specific: May need different frequencies for different muscles

### Fiber Type Distribution

**Two dimensions:**
- Between individuals: Some people are more fast-twitch dominant
- Between muscles: Same person has different distributions in different muscles

**Faster-twitch dominant:**
- Better response to heavy, low-rep training
- Longer recovery times
- Higher per-set growth potential

**Slower-twitch dominant:**
- Better response to high-rep training
- Faster recovery
- May need more volume to grow

### Training Age

**Definition:** Years of consistent, effective resistance training.

| Stage | Characteristics | Individualization Needs |
|-------|-----------------|------------------------|
| Beginner (<2 years) | Low MEV/MRV, technique developing | Focus on form, moderate variation |
| Intermediate (2-5 years) | Rising MEV/MRV, technique solid | More volume, more variation |
| Advanced (5+ years) | High MEV/MRV, mastered technique | Precision programming, high variation |

### Limb Lengths and Proportions

Affects exercise selection and technique:
- Long femurs → may favor leg press over squats for quads
- Long arms → may need wider grip on bench press
- Short torso → may excel at deadlifts

### Injury History

**Programming implications:**
- Avoid exercises that aggravate past injuries
- May need more warm-up for vulnerable areas
- Can require creative exercise substitutions

## Finding Individual Optima

### Volume Landmarks Discovery

Each person has unique MV, MEV, MAV, MRV for each muscle group. Find them through:

1. Start at estimated MEV
2. Track performance and recovery
3. Adjust based on feedback
4. Note where progress stalls (approaching MRV)
5. Note minimum for maintenance (MV)

### Frequency Discovery

Use the frequency-deriving algorithm:

1. Train muscle at session-MEV
2. Wait until fully recovered
3. Check if performance is at/above baseline
4. Yes → note interval (this is your frequency)
5. No → add one day next time

### Exercise Selection Discovery

For each exercise, note:
- Mind-muscle connection quality
- Strength progression over weeks
- Joint/connective tissue stress
- Soreness location and duration

Keep exercises with best SFR for your structure.

## Autoregulation

The practical implementation of individualization:

> "The best approach in programming is to make careful observations on recovery status... and plan and autoregulate Fatigue Management interventions accordingly."

### What to Monitor

- Performance (reps, loads achieved)
- Soreness (location, duration, intensity)
- Sleep quality and energy levels
- Motivation and psychological state
- Joint health and pain

### How to Adjust

| Signal | Adjustment |
|--------|------------|
| Performance declining | Reduce volume or intensity |
| Not sore at all | May increase volume |
| Crushing soreness | Reduce intensity or add rest |
| Great performance | May be able to push harder |
| Joint pain emerging | Modify exercise or reduce load |

## The Bell Curve of Responses

> "In hypertrophy training, there is bell curve of responses to various training principles, and all athletes over their careers lie somewhere on that curve."

Most people respond similarly to training principles, but:
- Some are on extremes of the curve
- Individual responses shift over career
- Circumstances change optimal approaches

## Key Quotes

> "Intelligent program design involves generating a reasonable training plan to start and then letting feedback from the athlete's responses drive individualized alterations to enhance it."

> "If one athlete benefits the most from 10 hours of weekly training, it's possible that some of their peers will benefit the most from something like 15 hours a week, but unlikely that they will need 50 hours."
