# Exercise Selection for Hypertrophy

**Type:** tactics
**Status:** authoritative
**Last Updated:** 2026-02-03
**Aliases:** Exercise Choice, Movement Selection, Compound vs Isolation

## Summary

Exercise selection for hypertrophy involves choosing movements that maximize growth stimulus while managing fatigue. The choice between compound and isolation exercises, exercise variety within a mesocycle, and biomechanical considerations all affect hypertrophy outcomes. Neither exclusively compound nor exclusively isolation approaches are optimal.

## Compound vs Isolation: Trade-offs

### Compound Movements

**Advantages:**
- Greater overall stimulus per set
- Higher forces possible (even at muscle level)
- Train multiple muscle groups simultaneously
- More time-efficient for total-body development

**Disadvantages:**
- Multiple muscles compete for recovery resources
- Supporting muscles may fatigue before target muscle
- Harder to isolate weak points

**Example:**
> "If you do squats, not only do you train your quads, but you train your glutes, your adductors, and your spinal erectors too. Because you can safely use more weight, the forces you can generate in compound movements, even when measured at the muscle level itself, are often higher with compounds than they are with isolation movements."

### Isolation Movements

**Advantages:**
- Target specific muscles or regions
- Add volume without taxing already-fatigued muscles
- Better mind-muscle connection possible
- Useful for addressing weak points

**Disadvantages:**
- Lower overall stimulus per set
- Less time-efficient
- May miss synergist development

**When to use:**
> "Isolations can be great when one muscle (let's say, your lower back) is approaching its maximum recoverable volume (MRV) but other muscles, such as the hamstrings, still need more volume. In that case, something like leg curls becomes a good choice to add hamstring volume without continuing to tax the other already-fatigued muscles."

## Why Multiple Exercises Are Needed

### Regional Hypertrophy

Different exercises target different regions within a muscle:
- Hack squats grow parts of quads that leg presses don't
- Different fiber pools activated by different movements
- Regional development requires exercise variety

> "Direct studies have found that doing training for one muscle group with a few different exercises per week causes more muscle growth than doing only one exercise per week, even if the number of sets is equated."

### Biomechanical Actions

Many muscles have multiple functions:
- Pectorals: pressing AND horizontal adduction
- Hamstrings: knee flexion AND hip extension
- Both actions should be trained for complete development

**Example:**
> "A good example is hamstring curls and hip-hinge movements (like the stiff-legged deadlift), which utilize different ranges of motion for the hamstrings. Using both variations is recommended because the hamstrings both flex the knee and extend the hip, and neither hip hinges nor leg curls do both at the same time."

### Motor Unit Diversity

Different exercises activate different motor unit pools:
- Some units activate at stretched positions
- Some at contracted positions
- Some through the middle range
- Full ROM exercises engage most, but variety helps

### Injury Prevention

Repetitive movement patterns increase wear-and-tear injury risk:
- Same stress patterns on connective tissue
- Alternating exercises reduces chronic irritation
- Example: alternate squats and leg presses to manage knee stress

## Specificity vs Variety Balance

### Too Little Variety (Under-application)

**The "just the basics" trap:**
- Only squatting for quads misses regional development
- Only pull-ups for back limits lat development
- "Hardcore" mentality often suboptimal for hypertrophy

### Too Much Variety (Over-application)

**The exercise-hopping trap:**
- Switching exercises too frequently disrupts directed adaptation
- Preparatory hypertrophy phase may not complete
- Some fibers never transition to myofibrillar hypertrophy

### Optimal Approach

1. Keep major exercises consistent within a mesocycle (4-8 weeks)
2. Use 2-4 exercises per muscle group per mesocycle
3. Include exercises covering different biomechanical actions
4. Vary exercises between mesocycles for regional development
5. Prioritize exercises with best SFR for your structure

## Exercise Selection Criteria

| Factor | Better Choice | Worse Choice |
|--------|---------------|--------------|
| Target muscle feel | Strong tension/burn | Feel in other muscles |
| ROM | Full, controlled | Partial, bouncing |
| Progression ability | Can add load/reps | Stuck at same weight |
| Joint health | No pain/discomfort | Chronic aches |
| Fatigue management | Recoverable | Excessive systemic fatigue |

## Key Quotes

> "Doing all of your training for a given muscle group with just one or two exercises indefinitely can lead to a higher chance of wear-and-tear injuries."

> "The sentiment that often leads to programs with very few exercises is the 'hardcore, just the basics, brother' attitude. But like most simplistic notions, this one falls short of being the best advice."

> "We should consider both [compound and isolation] as potential tools in a program designed to support specific goals based on a needs analysis, rather than favoring one over another as a matter of course."
