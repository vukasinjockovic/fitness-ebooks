# Training Methods for Hypertrophy

**Type:** tactics
**Status:** authoritative
**Last Updated:** 2026-02-03
**Aliases:** Training Modalities, Overload Techniques, Set Types

## Summary

Training methods are the different ways sets can be structured to apply hypertrophy stimuli. While straight sets should comprise the majority of training (~66-75% of total volume), various advanced methods can be strategically employed to emphasize specific stimuli like metabolite accumulation and cell swelling.

## Primary Method: Straight Sets

### Description
Perform a set of reps (within effective ranges), rest until ready for another productive set, and repeat.

### Characteristics
- Foundation of hypertrophy training
- Best for tension-based volume accumulation
- Allows best mind-muscle connection
- Easiest to track and progress

### Programming
- **2/3 to 3/4 of total volume** should be straight sets
- Rest: 30 seconds to 4 minutes (based on recovery)
- Any rep range within 5-30

### Rest Time Checklist
Before next set, verify:
1. Target muscle recovered to do at least 5 reps?
2. Synergist muscles recovered?
3. Nervous system recovered?
4. Cardiorespiratory system recovered?

## Secondary Method: Down Sets

### Description
Straight sets done with intentionally lighter load after heavier straight sets to stay in target rep range.

### When to Use
- When reps would drop below target range
- To maintain higher rep ranges after heavy work
- To accumulate more volume without excessive fatigue

### Example
- Sets 1-3: 175lb × 17, 13, 10 reps (target: 10-20)
- Sets 4-5: 155lb × 12, 10 reps (dropped to stay in range)

## Metabolite/Pump Emphasis Methods

### Giant Sets (Marathon Sets)

**Description:** Series of sets toward a total rep goal, constant load, without regard to reps per set.

**Example:** 50 total lateral raises with 20lb dumbbells, as many sets as needed.

**Best for:**
- Beginners (don't need accurate RIR)
- Exercises with poor load increments
- Time flexibility situations
- Mind-muscle connection focus

**Downside:** Difficult to track week-to-week

### Myoreps

**Description:** First set to near-failure (10-20 reps, 0-2 RIR), then short rest (few breaths), subsequent sets of 5-10 reps at 0-2 RIR, repeat.

**Purpose:** Maximize ratio of "effective reps" to total reps.

**Best for:**
- Isolation exercises only
- Exercises where local fatigue is limiting factor
- Time efficiency

**NOT for:**
- Compound movements
- Exercises limited by systemic/cardio fatigue
- Beginning of sessions

### Drop Sets

**Description:** Sets to 3 RIR or less, rest until burn fades, reduce weight, repeat for 5-10 reps.

**Comparison to Myoreps:**
- Similar concept but with load reduction
- Probably targets slower fibers slightly more
- Allows more total volume

**Best for:**
- End of workouts
- Isolation movements
- Pump/metabolite emphasis

### Pre-Exhaust Supersets

**Description:** Isolation movement for target muscle, then immediately compound movement involving that muscle.

**Example:** Triceps pushdowns → Close-grip push-ups

**Purpose:** Make target muscle the limiting factor in compound movement.

**Benefits:**
- More "effective reps" for target muscle
- Enhanced metabolite accumulation
- Better pump
- Improved mind-muscle connection

**Programming:**
- Isolation: 10-20 reps
- Compound: 5-10 reps
- Normal rest between supersets

### Non-Overlapping Supersets

**Description:** Two exercises back-to-back for different muscle groups (often antagonists).

**Example:** Leg presses → Stiff-legged deadlifts

**Best for:**
- Time-constrained training
- Higher systemic fatigue tolerance

**Downside:** Not ideal for mind-muscle connection

### Occlusion (Blood Flow Restriction) Training

**Description:** Bands around limbs to occlude blood flow, train with very light weights to failure.

**Benefits:**
- Robust growth effects from 20% 1RM loads
- Very short rest times possible
- Low systemic fatigue

**Best for:**
- Injury rehabilitation
- Deep calorie deficit phases
- Adding volume without joint stress

**Limitations:**
- Only works for limb muscles
- Probably less effective for fastest fibers
- Equipment/technique required

## Method Selection Summary

| Method | Primary Stimulus | Best Placement | Volume % |
|--------|------------------|----------------|----------|
| Straight Sets | Tension, Volume | First | 66-75% |
| Down Sets | Volume maintenance | After straight sets | As needed |
| Giant Sets | Volume, Pump | Flexible | 5-10% |
| Myoreps | Effective reps, Metabolites | End | 5-10% |
| Drop Sets | Metabolites, Pump | End | 5-10% |
| Supersets | Metabolites, Time efficiency | Middle-End | 10-15% |
| Occlusion | Metabolites, Low fatigue | Anytime | 5-10% |

## Programming Guidelines

1. **Start with straight sets** - They should dominate your program
2. **Don't overuse metabolite methods** - They cause high fatigue
3. **Match method to exercise** - Compounds → straight sets; Isolation → flexible
4. **Order matters** - Straight sets first, metabolite work last
5. **One method per muscle per session** - Don't stack multiple advanced methods

## Key Quotes

> "Straight sets—the most often used but least sexy hypertrophy training stimulus—involve performing a set of reps, resting until you are ready for another productive set, and repeating as needed."

> "An average program will often be composed of roughly between 2/3 to 3/4 (counting by total working sets done) straight sets, with the other training methods making up the rest."

> "As a general rule, compound, large-muscle-mass exercises in which the lifter has to support himself against gravity produce a lot of central, cardiorespiratory, and synergist fatigue relative to local fatigue, and thus are poor choices for myoreps."
