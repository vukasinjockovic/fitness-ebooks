# TDEE Framework (Total Daily Energy Expenditure)

**Type:** framework
**Status:** solid
**Last Updated:** 2026-02-03
**Aliases:** metabolic rate, net metabolic rate, daily calorie burn, metabolism

## Summary

Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE) represents the total calories burned per day through three components: Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR), Physical Activity Level (including NEAT), and Thermic Effect of Food (TEF). Understanding and calculating TDEE is essential for setting appropriate calorie targets for body recomposition.

## The Three Components

### 1. Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR) — 50-70% of TDEE
The calories burned at rest to maintain basic metabolic functions. If you sat on a couch all day doing nothing but breathing, this is roughly what you'd burn.

**Factors affecting BMR:**
- Gender
- Age
- Height
- Weight
- Body composition (lean mass requires more energy)
- Genetics
- Dietary history
- Gut microbiome
- Hormonal factors
- Ambient/body temperature

— Source: Body Recomposition, Ch.4

### 2. Physical Activity Level — 20-35% of TDEE
Calories burned through all movement, including:
- Formal exercise (lifting, cardio)
- NEAT (Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis)

**Key insight:** NEAT varies greatly between individuals and is largely responsible for massive differences in total metabolic rate between people of similar size.

— Source: Body Recomposition, Ch.4

### 3. Thermic Effect of Food (TEF) — ~10% of TDEE
Energy expended digesting, absorbing, and processing food.

| Macronutrient | Calories/gram | TEF Ranking |
|---------------|---------------|-------------|
| Fat | ~9 | Lowest |
| Carbohydrates | ~4 | Medium |
| Protein | ~4 | Highest |

**Implication:** High-protein diets burn more calories through digestion alone.

— Source: Body Recomposition, Ch.4

## TDEE Calculation

```
TDEE = BMR × Activity Multiplier
```

See [Activity Multipliers](../tactics/activity-multipliers.md) for specific values.

## Key Quotes

> "How many calories you burn in a day is determined by your body's baseline functions (BMR), your activity (both exercise and non-exercise (NEAT)) and even the types of foods you consume (TEF)."

> "When in a caloric deficit for an extended period of time, your body senses the decline in food intake and automatically decreases energy expenditure by downregulating NEAT."
