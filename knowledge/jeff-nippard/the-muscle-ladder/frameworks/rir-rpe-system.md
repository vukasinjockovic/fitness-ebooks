# RIR-RPE System

**Type:** framework
**Status:** solid
**Last Updated:** 2026-02-03
**Aliases:** Reps in Reserve, Rating of Perceived Exertion, RPE Scale

## Summary

The RIR-RPE system is a method for quantifying training effort by estimating proximity to muscular failure. RIR (Reps in Reserve) counts how many additional reps could theoretically be performed, while RPE (Rating of Perceived Exertion) uses a 1-10 scale mapped to RIR values. This system allows lifters to train at appropriate intensities for hypertrophy without always reaching absolute failure.

## The Scale

| RPE | RIR | Description |
|-----|-----|-------------|
| 10 | 0 | Maximal effort (failure) |
| 9.5 | 0 | No RIR but could increase load |
| 9 | 1 | 1 rep in reserve |
| 8.5 | 1-2 | Definitely 1, maybe 2 RIR |
| 8 | 2 | 2 reps in reserve |
| 7.5 | 2-3 | Definitely 2, maybe 3 RIR |
| 7 | 3 | 3 reps in reserve |
| 5-6 | 4-6 | Moderate effort |
| 3-4 | — | Light effort |
| 1-2 | — | Little to no effort |

## Recommended RPE by Exercise Type

| Exercise Type | Hypertrophy RPE | Notes |
|---------------|-----------------|-------|
| Primary compounds (squat, deadlift, bench) | 6-8 | Higher injury risk, more fatigue |
| Secondary compounds (pulldowns, lunges) | 8-10 | Moderate fatigue impact |
| Isolation exercises (curls, laterals) | 9-10 | Minimal risk, can push to failure |

## Key Insights

1. **The anabolic sweet spot is 0-3 RIR** - Most sets should be within this range for optimal hypertrophy
   — Source: The Muscle Ladder, Ch.6

2. **Failure not required** - Research shows 1-2 RIR produces similar hypertrophy to training to failure
   — Source: The Muscle Ladder, Ch.6 (citing 2024 Australian study)

3. **Failure training limits volume** - More fatigue = fewer total sets = potentially suboptimal growth
   — Source: The Muscle Ladder, Ch.6

4. **RPE accuracy improves with experience** - Requires calibration through failure sets and feedback
   — Source: The Muscle Ladder, Ch.6

## Strategies to Improve RPE Accuracy

1. Get more lifting experience
2. Occasionally push to failure to calibrate
3. Use anchoring sets (guess RPE, then verify by continuing to failure)
4. Record and review training videos
5. Get feedback from experienced coaches/partners

## Key Quotes

> "Most lifters—newbies and experienced trainees alike—stop sets long before they've pushed themselves hard enough to force their muscles to adapt."

> "You can't know when you're close to failure unless you sometimes push a set as hard as you can."

> "Going to failure too early or too often limits your volume and load, which means less benefit despite the higher effort."
