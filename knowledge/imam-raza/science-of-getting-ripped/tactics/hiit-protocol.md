# HIIT Protocol (High Intensity Interval Training)

**Type:** tactic
**Status:** solid
**Last Updated:** 2026-02-04
**Aliases:** Interval training, sprinting, sprint intervals

## Summary

HIIT alternates between intense effort (60 seconds at 9/10 intensity) and recovery periods. Unlike steady-state cardio that only burns calories during exercise, HIIT triggers the "afterburn effect" (EPOC), burning calories for up to 48 hours post-workout. The key is the "interval" part—switching between extremes of intense activity and rest.

## Basic Protocol

> "3 to 5 minute warm up
> intense running for 1 minute
> low intensity for 1 minute
> repeat 5-6 times"

— Source: Science of Getting Ripped, Training Principle 9

## Why HIIT Works

### Oxygen Debt Mechanism

> "Your anaerobic metabolism works without oxygen, and the by-product of that work is lactic acid. As lactic acid builds in your body, it creates a state of 'oxygen debt' in your body. During recovery periods, your heart and lungs work together to 'pay back' this oxygen debt."

### Energy System Training

HIIT trains both aerobic (oxygen-based) and anaerobic (glycolytic) systems:
- **Anaerobic:** Uses muscle glycogen for intense bursts
- **Aerobic:** Recovers oxygen debt, converts carbs to energy

> "So interval training (aka sprinting) gets you lean and in shape fast by improving oxygen utilization at the muscular level."

## Exercise Options

> "Other exercises you can do in an interval style:
> - Running Stairs
> - Jumping Rope
> - Hill Sprints
> - Jumping Exercises (box jumps, squat jumps, etc.)"

**Equipment options:**
- Stationary bike (recommended for beginners)
- Treadmill
- Track sprints
- Bodyweight movements

## Critical Success Factor

> "The key to making intervals work is the 'interval' part where you switch between the two extremes of intense activity and rest, otherwise you're doing a regular 'steady state' cardio workout. If you don't rest between the intervals you won't be able to sustain the intensity and subsequently won't get the benefits."

## HIIT vs Steady State Comparison

| Factor | Steady State Cardio | HIIT |
|--------|---------------------|------|
| Duration | 30-60+ minutes | 15-20 minutes |
| Intensity | Moderate (5-6/10) | High (9/10) bursts |
| Calorie burn timing | During only | During + 48 hours after |
| Muscle preservation | Poor (can burn muscle) | Good (preserves muscle) |
| Metabolic improvement | Moderate | High |

## Key Takeaways from the Book

> "Low intensity 'steady state' cardio burns calories while the activity is being performed"

> "Short, high-intensity interval training has been proved to burn calories for up to 48 hours after working out; meaning you can burn calories doing nothing"

> "Intervals can be done with any type of exercise; it's just characterized by 60 seconds of intense effort, and 60 seconds of moderate effort for 5-6 rounds"

## Muscle Preservation Warning

> "Suffice it to say, if you want to get lean and ripped, you have to sprint. That's because as your body burns fat, it could potentially burn muscle as well. Lifting weights and sprinting is critical to ensuring that your body burns fat and maintains muscle."

## Progression

> "There is no one best way to perform intervals, just start slow and progressively increase intensity."
