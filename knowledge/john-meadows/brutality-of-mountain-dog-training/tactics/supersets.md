# Supersets

**Type:** tactic
**Status:** solid
**Last Updated:** 2026-02-03
**Aliases:** Super Sets, Compound Sets, Pre-Exhaust

## Summary

Supersets involve performing one exercise immediately followed by another, typically for the same muscle group. While commonly used for pre-exhaustion (isolation before compound), Meadows encourages doing them in reverse order too - compound exercises followed by isolation can be "insanely painful and productive."

## How to Perform

1. Complete first exercise to failure/near failure
2. Immediately move to second exercise (no rest)
3. Complete second exercise to failure

## Two Approaches

### Traditional (Pre-Exhaust)
Isolation → Compound
- Example: Flyes before bench press
- Fatigues target muscle before assistance muscles limit you

### Reverse (Post-Exhaust)
Compound → Isolation
- Example: Squats followed by leg extensions
> "Doing leg extensions after squats can be insanely painful and productive!"
— Ch. 2

**Primary benefit:** Maximum activation pattern variety by coupling fatigue with different movements for the same muscle.

## Why Supersets Excel at Activation

> "Mountain Dog Training not only varies exercises on a regular basis, but also, specifically when doing supersets, creates a one-two punch by coupling fatigue with two or more different movements for the same muscle group."
— Ch. 2

## When to Use

- **Phase 3:** Supra-Maximal Pump exercises
- **Pump Workouts:** Often use supersets, trisets, or giant sets
- Whenever maximizing activation variety is the goal

## Advanced Variations

- **Trisets:** Three exercises in sequence
- **Giant Sets:** Four or more exercises in sequence
- Used especially on Pump workout days
