# Mechanical Tension

**Type:** concept
**Status:** solid
**Last Updated:** 2026-02-03
**Aliases:** Tensile Stress, Muscular Tension

## Summary

Mechanical tension is the primary stimulus that sets weight training apart from other forms of exercise. It refers to the force generated by muscles during contraction against resistance. In Mountain Dog Training, mechanical tension is maximized through Phase 2 (Explosive Exercises) and amplified through techniques like Iso-Holds that take advantage of muscles being stronger during isometric and eccentric contractions.

## Key Findings

1. **Correlation with size:** The stronger the bodybuilder, the larger their muscles (though not perfect correlation).
   — Source: Brutality of Mountain Dog Training, Ch. 2

2. **Optimal load range:** Heavy loads (75-90% 1RM, ~5-15 reps to failure) seem optimal for muscle growth in standard training.
   — Source: Brutality of Mountain Dog Training, Ch. 2

3. **Muscle primed for tensile stress:** Simply stretching developing muscle at rest triggers protein synthesis and signaling molecule production.
   — Source: Brutality of Mountain Dog Training, Ch. 2

4. **Intensification extends tension:** All MD Intensification Techniques prolong sets, creating greater "time under tension."
   — Source: Brutality of Mountain Dog Training, Ch. 2

5. **Motor unit recruitment:** The closer to muscular failure and greater the fatigue, the greater the number of motor units called into action.
   — Source: Brutality of Mountain Dog Training, Ch. 2

## Role in Mountain Dog Training

- **Phase 2 (Explosive):** Primary focus on mechanical tension through heavy compound movements
- **Iso-Holds:** Amplify tensile stimulus by exploiting isometric strength
- **Bands/Chains:** Increase loading throughout the full range of motion
