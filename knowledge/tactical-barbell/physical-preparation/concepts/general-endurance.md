# General Endurance (GE)
**Type:** concept
**Status:** solid
**Last Updated:** 2026-02-03
**Source:** Tactical Barbell: Physical Preparation for Law Enforcement (2015) by K. Black
**Aliases:** GE, Long Steady State, LSS, Cardio, Roadwork

## Summary
General Endurance (GE) refers to the ability to perform low-intensity physical activity for extended periods. It's commonly known as "cardio" or "roadwork" and is used to develop aerobic capacity. In Tactical Barbell, GE is considered essential for tactical athletes despite some fitness circles dismissing steady-state cardio.

## Definition
> "General Endurance is responsible for your ability to perform low-intensity physical activity for lengthy periods of time. It's what we commonly think of as 'cardio', or 'roadwork' if you're a boxer."
— Source: Chapter 5, "Conditioning"

## Benefits for Tactical Athletes
- Improves work capacity (ability to work harder for longer)
- Contributes to anaerobic system efficiency
- Builds foundation for high-intensity work

> "If you have a well-developed general endurance base, then you'll be able to work harder for longer periods of time."
— Source: Chapter 5

## Implementation in PPLE
- **Block I:** 2 GE sessions per week (Days 2 and 4)
- **Block II:** 1 GE session per week (Day 6)
- Typical duration: 20-45 minutes steady state running
- Intensity: Conversational pace ("a pace you can still somewhat comfortably hold a conversation at")
— Source: Chapters 5, 7-10

## The LSS Debate
The book addresses the controversy around steady-state cardio:

> "There is a school of thought that believes you can get all the 'cardio' you need from short high intensity intervals as opposed to jogging for an hour. For the tactical athlete this is erroneous thinking."
— Source: Chapter 5

### Supporting Evidence
- References Joel Jamieson's "Ultimate MMA Conditioning"
- Notes that fighters with both roadwork and intervals outperform those who only do intervals
— Source: Chapter 5

## Running Tips
When struggling to maintain continuous running:
1. Instead of stopping, slow to a shuffle (running pace slower than walking)
2. This maintains the "never give up/never stop" mindset
3. After catching breath, lengthen stride and continue
— Source: Chapter 8, Session 2
