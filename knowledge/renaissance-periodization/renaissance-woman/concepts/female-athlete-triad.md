# Female Athlete Triad (FAT)

**Type:** Concept
**Status:** Solid
**Source:** Renaissance Woman, Chapter 6
**Authors:** Dr. Jen Case, Dr. Melissa Davis, Dr. Mike Israetel

## Summary

A potentially serious condition affecting female athletes characterized by the interplay of three factors: low energy availability, menstrual dysfunction, and decreased bone mineral density. While uncommon in full severity, partial manifestation is relatively common during aggressive dieting phases.

## The Triad Components

### 1. Energy Availability (Low Calorie Intake)
- Chronic hypocaloric dieting
- Insufficient calories to support athletic AND reproductive function
- Often combined with high training volume

### 2. Menstrual Function
- **Healthy:** Regular menstruation (eumenorrhea)
- **Impaired:** Irregular menstruation (oligomenorrhea)
- **Severe:** Absent menstruation (amenorrhea)

### 3. Bone Mineral Density (BMD)
- **Healthy:** Normal BMD for age
- **Impaired:** Osteopenia (low bone mass)
- **Severe:** Osteoporosis (very low bone mass, fragile bones)

## Pathophysiology

```
Low Energy Intake
      ↓
Body detects "starvation"
      ↓
Reproductive system pauses
(evolutionary energy conservation)
      ↓
Reduced estrogen production
      ↓
Decreased bone growth/repair signaling
      ↓
Net bone loss over time
```

**Critical insight:** Estrogen signals bone-building cells (osteoblasts) to build and repair bone. Low estrogen = bone degradation exceeds regeneration.

## Risk Factors

### Primary Triggers
- Body fat chronically below ~15%
- Prolonged hypocaloric dieting (>12 weeks without maintenance)
- Diet + high training volume combination

### Protective Factors
- Following 3-month max diet phases
- Maintaining reasonable body fat in off-season
- Adequate nutrient intake (especially calcium)

## Bone Loss: The Irreversible Consequence

> "Most research indicates that the bone loss that leads (first to osteopenia and then osteoporosis) is not reversible. Even if the causative factors of bone loss are eliminated, there doesn't seem to be much we can do to re-build what was lost."

**Implications:**
- Prevention is the only real solution
- Bone loss accumulates over years
- Each period of amenorrhea contributes
- Damage may not manifest until age 55+

## Who's at Risk?

### Higher Risk
- Women who diet aggressively for long periods
- Athletes in aesthetic sports (figure, gymnastics)
- Women maintaining very low body fat long-term
- Those with genetic predisposition to osteoporosis
- Caucasian and Asian women (statistically higher osteoporosis rates)

### Lower Risk
- Women following structured diet phases
- Those who maintain menstruation most of year
- Heavy training athletes (lifting builds bone)
- Good genetics for bone density

## Prevention Strategies

| Strategy | Implementation |
|----------|----------------|
| Limit diet duration | 3 months max before maintenance |
| Control deficit size | Never exceed 1000 cal/day deficit |
| Minimize low BF time | Contest lean only when necessary |
| Monitor menstruation | Spotty is okay; 3+ months absent = concern |
| Manage training fatigue | Deloads, rest days, active recovery |
| Calcium intake | Minimum 1200mg/day |
| Heavy training | Stimulates bone growth/preservation |

## Menstrual Expectations

**Normal variance:**
- Spotty/missing periods during final weeks of hard diet
- Brief disruption at very low body fat is expected
- Should return with maintenance phase

**Warning signs:**
- No period for 3+ consecutive months
- Persistent disruption outside of competition prep
- Amenorrhea despite returning to normal calories

## Late Menarche Concern

**Definition:** First menstruation after age 16

**Risk factors:**
- Excessive training/dieting during teen years
- Insufficient caloric intake during development

**Consequence:** Major missed window for bone building in teens → strongly associated with osteoporosis later in life

**For mothers/coaches:** Monitor young female athletes for this pattern

## The 15% Body Fat Threshold

While individual, ~15% body fat appears to be a critical threshold:
- Below: Reproductive function often impaired
- Above: Most women maintain normal menstruation

**Practical application:**
- Contest body fat (8-12%): Temporary only
- 15-18%: Lean but usually safe
- 20%+: Comfortable for long-term health

**Transition math:**
- 150 lb woman at 10% BF = 135 lb LBM
- At 20% BF = 169 lbs
- Difference = ~19 lbs
- Easily achievable in 12-week cut
